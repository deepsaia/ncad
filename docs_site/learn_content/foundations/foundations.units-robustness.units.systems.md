A **unit system** fixes a set of reference quantities against which every physical measurement is expressed. The modern scientific standard is the **International System of Units (SI)**, built on seven base units, chief among them the metre (length), kilogram (mass), and second (time). Since the 2019 redefinition, each base unit is anchored to an exact numerical value of a fundamental constant rather than an artefact: the metre, for example, follows from fixing the speed of light \( c = 299\,792\,458 \) m/s. Every other quantity is a **derived unit** formed as a product of powers of base units, and SI is *coherent*, meaning derived units carry no extra numerical factors, e.g.

\[ 1\,\text{N} = 1\,\frac{\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}, \qquad 1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\frac{\text{N}}{\text{m}^2} = 1\,\frac{\text{kg}}{\text{m}\cdot\text{s}^2}. \]

Alongside SI, the **imperial** and **US customary** systems remain widespread in manufacturing, expressing length in inches and feet, mass/force in pounds, and so on. Since 1959 the international inch is defined *exactly* as \( 1\,\text{in} = 25.4\,\text{mm} \), which makes inch-to-millimetre conversion exact rather than approximate; the older US survey foot differs by two parts per million and is a classic source of subtle error. Because both families remain in daily use, conversion is unavoidable, and a conversion factor is meaningful only when both the source and target units are explicitly known.

## Model units

Geometric models rarely store physically dimensioned quantities. Instead a modeler holds bare floating-point coordinates and attaches a single **model unit** (a unit-per-length) as document metadata: the number `10.0` means 10 mm, 10 in, or 10 m depending only on that declaration. This separation keeps the geometry kernel purely numeric and unit-agnostic, but it makes the unit declaration part of the model's contract. Importing geometry authored in inches into a millimetre document without applying the \( \times 25.4 \) scale silently yields a part that is 25.4 times too small, with no error raised because the numbers themselves are all valid.

Unit discipline is therefore a correctness concern, not mere bookkeeping. The 1999 loss of the Mars Climate Orbiter, traced to one subsystem emitting impulse in pound-force-seconds while another consumed newton-seconds, is the canonical reminder that a missing or mismatched unit label can be catastrophic even when every individual computation is arithmetically correct. Robust systems address this by carrying units as explicit, first-class data through every interface, converting at well-defined boundaries, and preferring a single coherent internal representation (commonly SI or millimetres) so that only import/export layers deal with conversion.

A related pitfall is the treatment of derived quantities under scaling. Lengths scale linearly with the unit change, but areas scale with its square and volumes with its cube, so a length conversion factor \( k \) implies mass/inertia scaling by \( k^3 \) (at fixed density) and section properties by higher powers. Any transform that rescales a model must propagate these consistently, which is exactly the reasoning captured formally by dimensional analysis.
