**Dimensional analysis** studies physical relationships through the *dimensions* of the quantities involved rather than their numerical values. Every mechanical quantity can be expressed as a product of powers of a small set of base dimensions, conventionally written with brackets: length \([L]\), mass \([M]\), and time \([T]\) (with \([\Theta]\) for temperature, \([I]\) for current, etc., when needed). A velocity has dimension \([L T^{-1}]\), a force \([M L T^{-2}]\), a pressure \([M L^{-1} T^{-2}]\). These dimensions are invariant under any choice of unit within a system, which is precisely what makes them a useful abstraction over the concrete numbers a model stores.

## Dimensional homogeneity

The governing principle is **dimensional homogeneity**: any equation expressing a physical law must have identical dimensions on both sides, and only quantities of like dimension may be added or compared. This is not a stylistic convention but a consequence of the requirement that physics be independent of the observer's unit choices. Homogeneity gives a cheap, powerful sanity check: if a derived formula produces \([L]\) on one side and \([L T^{-1}]\) on the other, it is wrong regardless of the arithmetic. It also constrains the form of admissible laws, since a term like \( \sin(x) \) or \( e^{x} \) requires its argument \( x \) to be **dimensionless**.

The formal engine behind this is the **Buckingham \( \pi \) theorem**. If a physical relationship involves \( n \) variables built from \( k \) independent base dimensions, then it can be re-expressed in terms of \( p = n - k \) independent dimensionless groups:

\[ f(q_1, q_2, \dots, q_n) = 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad F(\pi_1, \pi_2, \dots, \pi_{p}) = 0. \]

Each \( \pi_i \) is a product of powers of the original variables chosen so that all dimensions cancel. Familiar examples are the Reynolds number \( \mathrm{Re} = \rho v L / \mu \) and the Mach number; because they are pure numbers, two geometrically similar systems with matching dimensionless groups behave identically, which is the theoretical basis for scale-model testing and for deriving scaling laws without solving the full governing equations.

In engineering software, dimensional analysis underwrites automated **consistency checking**. A parametric expression such as `wall_thickness = pressure * radius / stress` can be validated at authoring time by tracking the dimension of each operand: multiplication and division combine dimension exponents additively, addition and subtraction require matching dimensions, and transcendental functions require dimensionless arguments. When these rules are enforced, a whole class of specification errors, mixed-unit inputs and physically meaningless formulas, is caught before any geometry is built or any solver is invoked. The technique complements unit tracking: units guard the numerical conversion, while dimensions guard the structural validity of the relationships themselves.
