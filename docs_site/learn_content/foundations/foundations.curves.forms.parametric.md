A curve is represented in **parametric form** when its points are generated by a vector-valued function of a single scalar parameter. In three dimensions,

\[ \mathbf{C}(u) = \bigl(x(u),\, y(u),\, z(u)\bigr), \qquad u \in [a, b]. \]

Each spatial coordinate is its own function of the parameter \(u\), and as \(u\) sweeps its interval the point \(\mathbf{C}(u)\) traces out the curve. The parameter is a purely internal bookkeeping variable: it orders the points and gives a natural notion of "traveling along" the curve, but it need not correspond to any physical quantity such as distance or time.

The decisive advantage of the parametric form is **generative power**. To draw, tessellate, or sample a curve you simply evaluate \(\mathbf{C}(u)\) at a sequence of parameter values; no equation-solving is required. Because each coordinate is an independent function of \(u\), the form imposes no restriction that a given \(x\) map to a single \(y\). It therefore represents vertical tangents, closed loops, self-intersections, and fully three-dimensional space curves with equal ease, which is precisely why it is the representation of choice for geometric modeling. The polynomial and rational spline families that dominate design work, including Bezier, B-spline, and non-uniform rational B-spline (NURBS) curves, are all parametric.

## Derivatives, speed, and reparameterization

Differentiating with respect to the parameter gives the tangent (velocity) vector \(\mathbf{C}'(u)\), whose direction is tangent to the curve and whose magnitude \(\lVert \mathbf{C}'(u) \rVert\) is the **parametric speed**. This distinction matters: the geometric shape of the curve is the image set \(\{\mathbf{C}(u)\}\), while the speed describes how that image is traversed. A curve is called **regular** where \(\mathbf{C}'(u) \neq \mathbf{0}\), the condition that guarantees a well-defined tangent direction.

A given curve admits infinitely many parameterizations: substituting any smooth, monotonic function \(u = \phi(t)\) yields \(\tilde{\mathbf{C}}(t) = \mathbf{C}(\phi(t))\), which retraces the identical geometry at a different speed. The canonical choice that removes this ambiguity is the **arc-length (natural) parameterization** \(\mathbf{C}(s)\), defined so that \(\lVert \mathbf{C}'(s) \rVert \equiv 1\); it is the reference frame in which most differential-geometric quantities take their simplest form. Arc length itself is recovered from any parameterization by the integral

\[ s(u) = \int_{a}^{u} \lVert \mathbf{C}'(t) \rVert \, dt. \]

Rational parametric forms extend the picture by writing coordinates as ratios of polynomials, \(x(u) = X(u)/W(u)\) and so on. This is what allows exact representation of conics (circles, ellipses, parabolas, hyperbolas) that no ordinary polynomial curve can capture, and it is the reason the "R" in NURBS is present. The price is that reparameterization and differentiation involve the quotient rule, but the expressive gain, exact circular arcs and true perspective invariance, is essential to engineering geometry.
