The MuJoCo XML format (MJCF) describes a physical system as a **kinematic tree**: a hierarchy of rigid **bodies** rooted at a special `world` body. Each body carries a spatial frame defined relative to its parent (a position and an orientation, the latter expressible as a quaternion, axis-angle, Euler angles, or a Z-X rotation). Because pose is expressed relative to the parent, the tree encodes the assembly structure directly, and the global pose of every body is recovered each step by composing frames from the root outward (forward kinematics). Bodies are the load-bearing element of the model: everything with mass, degrees of freedom, or collision shape hangs off a body.

A body's degrees of freedom come from its **joints**, and MuJoCo uses an unusual but powerful convention: a joint *adds* motion freedom between a body and its parent rather than *connecting* two independent bodies. A body with **no** joint is rigidly welded to its parent (zero relative DOF); joints are added to introduce motion. The primitive joint types are `free` (6 DOF floating base), `ball` (3 rotational DOF), `hinge` (1 revolute DOF), and `slide` (1 prismatic DOF). Several joints can be stacked on one body to compose, for example, a 2-DOF universal joint from two hinges. This yields a **minimal-coordinate** (reduced-coordinate) formulation in which the tree has exactly the intended freedoms, with no algebraic drift to correct.

The configuration is stored in two vectors whose lengths differ because of orientation representation. The position vector `qpos` has size `nq`, while the velocity vector `qvel` has size `nv`; for scalar joints they match, but a `ball` joint uses a 4-number unit quaternion for 3 DOF and a `free` joint uses 7 numbers (3 translation + 4 quaternion) for 6 DOF, so \( n_q > n_v \) whenever such joints appear. Velocities and accelerations always live in the `nv`-dimensional tangent space, and quaternion states are integrated on the manifold rather than by naive component addition.

Every body has an **inertial** element describing its mass distribution: total mass \(m\), the location of the center of mass, and a \(3\times 3\) rotational inertia tensor, conventionally given as a diagonal in the body's principal-axis frame. If the inertial element is omitted, the compiler infers mass properties from the body's collision **geoms** and a specified material density, integrating the primitive shapes analytically. These spatial inertias populate the mass matrix \(M(q)\) that drives the equations of motion.

**Geoms** are the geometric primitives attached to a body: `plane`, `sphere`, `capsule`, `ellipsoid`, `cylinder`, `box`, convex `mesh`, and `hfield` (height field). They serve both collision detection and rendering, and carry contact parameters (friction, compliance) plus collision-filtering bitmasks (`contype`/`conaffinity`) that decide which pairs are even tested. Massless **sites** provide named reference frames for sensors, actuators, and tendon routing without affecting dynamics. Together these elements let the minimal-coordinate equations of motion be assembled and evaluated:

\[ M(q)\,\dot v + c(q, v) = \tau + J^{\top} f, \]

where \(c\) collects Coriolis, centrifugal, gravitational, and passive forces, \(\tau\) is applied/actuator force, and \(J^{\top} f\) injects constraint forces.
