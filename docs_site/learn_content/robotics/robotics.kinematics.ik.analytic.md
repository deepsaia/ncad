Inverse kinematics (IK) reverses the forward map: given a desired end-effector pose \( T_d \in SE(3) \), find the joint values \( q \) that place the tool there. Analytic (closed-form) IK solves this with explicit algebraic formulas rather than iteration. When a solution exists in closed form it is enormously valuable: it is fast (a handful of trigonometric evaluations), it returns *all* solution branches at once, and it never fails to converge because there is nothing to converge. This is why closed-form IK is the preferred method whenever the mechanism admits it, and why industrial arms are frequently designed on purpose so that it does.

Unlike the forward map, IK is generally multi-valued and sometimes has no solution at all. A target outside the reachable workspace has zero solutions; a target inside it typically has several. A common six-axis arm has up to eight distinct postures reaching the same pose, usually described in three binary families: shoulder left/right, elbow up/down, and wrist flip/no-flip. Each family corresponds to a different sign choice made while solving, and any control system must decide which branch to use (often the one closest to the current configuration or the one that avoids joint limits and singularities).

## The kinematic-decoupling condition

The key enabler for closed-form solutions of six-DOF arms is a design feature called a spherical wrist: the axes of the last three joints intersect at a single point, the wrist center. When this holds, the problem *decouples*. The wrist-center position depends only on the first three joints, so

\[ p_c = p_d - d_6\, R_d\, \hat{z}, \]

where \( p_d, R_d \) are the desired tool position and orientation and \( d_6 \) is the tool offset along the final axis. One solves the first three joints from the position \( p_c \) using planar trigonometry (law of cosines for the elbow, an arctangent for the shoulder), then solves the last three joints from the residual orientation \( R_{wrist} = R_{0,3}^{\top} R_d \) by extracting Euler-like angles. Pieper's result guarantees that any six-DOF chain with three consecutive intersecting axes (or three parallel axes) is solvable in closed form, which is precisely why the spherical wrist is nearly universal on articulated arms.

The algebra typically relies on a small toolkit: the two-argument arctangent \( \mathrm{atan2}(y,x) \) to recover angles over the full circle with correct quadrant, the law of cosines to invert triangle geometry, and half-angle or Weierstrass substitutions to reduce equations of the form \( a\cos\theta + b\sin\theta = c \) to polynomials. The \( \pm \) roots of those polynomials are exactly the multiple postures. When a manipulator lacks the intersecting- or parallel-axis structure, no general closed form exists and one falls back to numerical IK, though special-purpose closed-form solvers can still be generated for many specific geometries.

Analytic IK has limits worth stating plainly. It is derived per kinematic structure, so changing the geometry means re-deriving the equations. It does not gracefully handle redundant arms (seven or more joints), where the solution set is a continuum rather than a finite list. And it says nothing by itself about joint limits, obstacle avoidance, or singularity proximity; those constraints must be applied afterward to select among the discrete branches it returns.
