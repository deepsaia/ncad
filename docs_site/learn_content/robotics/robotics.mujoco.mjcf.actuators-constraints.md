**Actuators** are the interface between control inputs and the mechanical system: they convert an entry of the control vector `ctrl` into a generalized force applied to the tree. MuJoCo models an actuator as three composable pieces. A **transmission** selects what the actuator acts on and supplies a moment arm that maps scalar actuator force into generalized force, whether that target is a joint, a tendon, a slider-crank, a site (Cartesian force), or a body. Optional **activation dynamics** give the actuator internal state \(a\) that lags the command, modeling first-order filters, pneumatics, or biological muscle. Finally a **gain/bias** stage produces the scalar force. The general form is

\[ F = \text{gain}(\ell, \dot\ell, a)\,\cdot u + \text{bias}(\ell, \dot\ell, a), \]

where \(\ell\) is transmission length and \(u\) the input; the total is accumulated into `qfrc_actuator` through the moment arms. Named shortcuts specialize this template: `motor` (direct force/torque), `position` and `velocity` (built-in P and D servo terms via affine bias), `muscle`, `cylinder` (pneumatic/hydraulic), `intvelocity` (integrated velocity), and `adhesion` (a suction-like normal force at contacts).

**Tendons** are scalar length quantities that let a single actuator, spring, or limit act across multiple joints or along a routed path. A **fixed tendon** is a linear combination of scalar joint coordinates, \( \ell = \sum_i c_i\, q_i \); this compactly expresses gearing, coupled fingers, or differentials. A **spatial tendon** is the minimum-length path of a physical cable threaded through an ordered list of **sites**, optionally wrapping around sphere or cylinder obstacle geoms and passing through **pulleys** that split it into branches. Tendons can carry passive stiffness and damping, enforce length limits, and serve as an actuator transmission, which is what makes cable-driven and musculoskeletal mechanisms tractable.

The kinematic tree, by construction, can only represent open chains. **Equality constraints** lift that restriction by imposing holonomic relationships on top of the tree, which is how closed loops and inter-joint couplings are modeled. The main types are `connect` (pin a point on one body to a point on another, a ball joint), `weld` (fix the full relative pose of two bodies), `joint` (couple two scalar joints through a quartic polynomial, e.g. a gear ratio), `tendon` (couple two tendon lengths), and `flex`/`distance` variants. Each constraint contributes a residual \( r(q) \) that should vanish, with constraint Jacobian \( J = \partial r / \partial q \).

Crucially, equality constraints are **not** enforced by projection or Lagrange elimination; they are handed to the same soft-constraint solver that handles contacts, appearing as additional rows in the constraint system \( J^{\top} f \). Their reference behavior and stiffness are set by the `solref`/`solimp` compliance parameters, so a `connect` constraint behaves like a stiff spring pulling two points together rather than an exact algebraic identity. This unification means a single solver resolves contacts, friction, joint limits, tendon limits, and loop-closure constraints together, at the cost of small, tunable constraint violation.

In practice this trio covers the mechanisms a minimal-coordinate tree cannot express on its own: four-bar and parallel linkages (via `connect`/`weld`), geartrains and mimic joints (via `joint` equalities or fixed tendons), and cable- or muscle-actuated systems (via spatial tendons as actuator transmissions). Because everything reduces to Jacobians and forces in a common framework, these features compose cleanly and remain differentiable for gradient-based control and identification.
