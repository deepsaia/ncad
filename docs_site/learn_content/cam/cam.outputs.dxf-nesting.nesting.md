Nesting is the optimization step that decides how to arrange many two-dimensional part outlines on the available stock — sheets, coils, or plates — before cutting. Because raw material is often the dominant cost in a fabricated part and stock comes in fixed sizes, the difference between a naive layout and a good nest is a direct, recurring economic effect. Nesting exists to squeeze the most usable parts out of each sheet, minimize the number of sheets consumed, respect the machine bed and remnant sizes, and produce a cut order that reduces cycle time. It is the bridge between the per-part profiles produced by flat-pattern/profile export and the production job that a cutting machine will run.

Formally, nesting is a member of the family of **cutting and packing** problems. Placing rectangles on a strip is the strip-packing problem; filling fixed sheets is two-dimensional bin packing; the classic industrial statement is the **cutting-stock problem**. When parts are irregular (true silhouettes rather than bounding boxes) it is the *irregular strip/bin packing* or **nesting problem** proper. All of these are NP-hard: the number of feasible placements explodes combinatorially, so exact optimization is intractable at production scale and practical systems rely on constructive heuristics and metaheuristics. The natural objective is material **utilization**, the ratio of placed part area to consumed stock area,

\[ U = \frac{\sum_i A_i}{L \cdot W}, \]

for a sheet of length \(L\) and width \(W\); equivalently one minimizes trim loss, sheet count, or total cut length depending on what dominates cost.

## The geometry of overlap-free placement

The core geometric primitive that makes true-shape nesting tractable is the **No-Fit Polygon (NFP)**. Given two polygons \(A\) (fixed) and \(B\) (orbiting), the NFP is the locus traced by \(B\)'s reference point as \(B\) slides around \(A\) staying in contact but never overlapping. The key property is a clean feasibility test: \(A\) and \(B\) do **not** overlap if and only if \(B\)'s reference point lies on or outside \(NFP_{AB}\), and they touch exactly when it lies on the boundary. The companion **Inner-Fit Polygon** does the same job for containment inside the sheet boundary. Replacing expensive per-placement intersection tests with a point-in-polygon query against precomputed NFPs is what lets a nester evaluate thousands of candidate positions quickly. When high packing density is not required, parts are approximated by their bounding rectangles and the problem collapses to far cheaper rectangular packing, at the cost of the wasted area inside each concave part.

Because the problem is NP-hard, solvers combine a **placement rule** with a **search strategy**. Bottom-left-fill (drop each part as far down and left as it will go) and first-fit-decreasing (place largest parts first) are the workhorse constructive rules; simulated annealing, genetic algorithms, and tabu search then reorder the part sequence and adjust orientations to escape local optima. The allowed rotation set is a modeling choice — free rotation gives the tightest nests, while fixed increments (0/90/180) or a locked orientation are imposed when the material has a **grain or rolling direction** that constrains subsequent bending, or when anisotropic finish must stay aligned.

## Manufacturing constraints that shape a real nest

A geometrically valid nest is not yet a manufacturable one. Every contour must be inflated by half the **kerf** so the finished part lands on nominal dimension, and adjacent parts need a minimum part-to-part gap plus an edge margin from the sheet border; thermal processes (laser, plasma) require extra spacing to avoid heat build-up and edge quality loss. Advanced nests exploit **common-line cutting**, where two parts share a single cut to halve edge length and pierces, and add **micro-joints or bridges** so small parts do not drop into the slats mid-job. The cutting technology also dictates layout topology: shears and some saws demand **guillotine** (edge-to-edge, recursively decomposable) cuts, whereas laser/plasma/waterjet permit arbitrary **non-guillotine** free cuts and therefore denser nests. Finally, offcut and **remnant management** — cutting so that leftover material forms a reusable rectangle rather than confetti — turns short-term utilization into long-term yield. Getting these constraints into the objective, rather than fixing violations after the fact, is what separates a demonstration nester from one trusted to quote and run production.
