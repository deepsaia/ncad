A **tool holder** is the interface that grips the cutter and locates it in the spindle. It matters as much as the cutter itself because it sets the tool's position, its rigidity, and its reach. Common holder classes include steep-taper interfaces (7:24 tapers such as the BT and CAT families) that seat on the taper alone, the **HSK hollow-taper interface with simultaneous face contact** standardized internationally for high-speed and high-precision work, collet chucks, and shrink-fit holders. Face-and-taper contact (as in HSK) constrains the tool axially and radially at once, which improves repeatability and stiffness at high spindle speeds where centrifugal taper expansion would otherwise loosen a pure taper.

The key length parameter is the **gauge length** (measured from the holder's gauge line, the defined seating reference on the spindle interface, to the tool tip or to a datum on the holder). The controller's **tool-length offset** is set so that programmed Z corresponds to the tool tip regardless of how far the tool protrudes. The distance the cutter projects beyond the holder is the **overhang** or **stickout**, and it is the single most important lever on rigidity.

Overhang governs deflection because a milling tool behaves like a cantilever beam. For an end load \(F\) on a cantilever of length \(L\), flexural rigidity \(EI\), the tip deflection is

\[ \delta = \frac{F L^{3}}{3 E I}, \qquad I = \frac{\pi d^{4}}{64}, \]

so deflection scales with the **cube of overhang** and inversely with the **fourth power of shank diameter**. Doubling stickout multiplies deflection roughly eightfold; this is why the guiding rule is to hold the tool as short as the geometry allows and to prefer the largest shank that fits. Deflection shows up as dimensional error, tapered walls, and, dynamically, as regenerative **chatter** when the cutting frequency approaches the tool-holder assembly's natural frequency.

Two further holder properties are decisive at speed and for access. **Runout** (total indicated radial error at the tool) unevenly loads the flutes so that some cut thicker chips than others, accelerating wear and degrading finish; it accumulates across the spindle-holder-tool stack. **Balance** matters because rotating unbalance produces a force growing with the square of speed, so high-speed holders are balanced to a grade. Finally, the assembled holder-plus-tool profile, not just the cutter, is what a simulation must check for collision: the shank, holder body, and spindle nose must all clear the workpiece and fixtures throughout the toolpath, which is why the reachable region is defined by the whole assembly's silhouette and its gauge length.
