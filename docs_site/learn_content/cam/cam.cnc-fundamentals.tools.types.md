A cutting tool's **cutting-profile geometry** determines both what surfaces it can produce and how efficiently it removes material. The common milling and hole-making tool families differ mainly in the shape of the cutting envelope swept by the flutes and end. Choosing among them is a geometric and mechanical trade-off, not a matter of taste: the profile decides achievable corner radii, floor flatness, surface finish, and permissible feed.

Among milling cutters, a **flat (square) end mill** has a flat bottom and sharp corner; it produces flat floors and vertical walls and is the workhorse for prismatic pocketing and 2.5D work, but its sharp corner is a stress concentration prone to chipping. A **ball nose** end mill terminates in a hemisphere of radius equal to half the tool diameter; it is the standard for 3D sculptured surfaces because a sphere contacts any surface orientation, but it leaves a scalloped floor and its cutting speed falls to zero at the tip. A **bull nose (toroidal, corner-radius)** end mill is a compromise, a flat end with a rounded corner of radius \(r < D/2\); it gives a stronger corner than a square mill and a flatter floor than a ball, and is favored for roughing and for finishing shallow surfaces. A **face mill** is a large-diameter body with indexable inserts for high-throughput facing of flat surfaces. A **chamfer/spot tool** is a conical point (commonly 90 degrees included) used to break edges, spot-drill, and countersink.

Hole-making tools form their own family. A **twist drill** has a conical point (typically 118 or 135 degrees) and helical flutes to produce round holes; it is a plunging tool, not a side-cutting one. A **tap** cuts or forms internal threads and must be fed in exact synchrony with spindle rotation (feed per revolution equals the thread pitch), a constraint that distinguishes rigid tapping. Reamers, counterbores, and center drills round out the hole-making set for finishing diameter, seating fasteners, and starting accurate holes.

Geometrically each tool is parameterized by a small set of numbers that a toolpath and simulation consume directly: diameter \(D\), corner radius \(r\), taper angle, tip/point angle, flute count, helix angle, and overall and flute lengths. The **cutting-envelope** shape follows from these. For a ball nose finishing a surface with stepover \(s\), the residual **scallop (cusp) height** left between adjacent passes on a flat is

\[ h = R - \sqrt{R^{2} - \left(\tfrac{s}{2}\right)^{2}} \;\approx\; \frac{s^{2}}{8R}, \qquad R=\frac{D}{2}, \]

which shows why finish quality scales with tool radius and with the square of stepover. The same envelope model lets the planner test whether a chosen tool can reach into a concave fillet (its corner radius must not exceed the part's) and whether the floor of a pocket will be flat (only a flat or bull tool with sufficient radius clearance achieves that).
