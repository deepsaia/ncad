**Stock** (the *raw piece* or *blank*) is the solid volume of material present before machining begins. Every subtractive process is defined as material removal from stock, so the stock model is the reference against which cut volume, remaining material, tool engagement, and rest machining are all computed. Without an explicit stock model a toolpath cannot know where material actually is: it would either cut air (wasting time) or plunge into uncut bulk (breaking tools). The stock is a first-class geometric input to process planning, not a cosmetic backdrop.

Stock is typically defined in one of four ways. A **box** (rectangular prism) is the common case for prismatic parts sawn from plate or bar; it is usually derived from the part's axis-aligned bounding box in the work coordinate system. A **cylinder** models bar or tube stock for turning and for parts machined from round billet. **From-body** takes an existing solid, most often a near-net casting, forging, or the result of a prior operation, as the starting shape so that only the small machining allowance is removed. An **offset** grows a base shape outward by a uniform or per-face allowance to represent oversize raw material and finishing stock.

The box case reduces to an axis-aligned bounding box (AABB). Over the part vertices \(\{\mathbf{v}_i\}\), the extents are

\[ \mathbf{p}_{\min}=\big(\min_i x_i,\ \min_i y_i,\ \min_i z_i\big),\qquad \mathbf{p}_{\max}=\big(\max_i x_i,\ \max_i y_i,\ \max_i z_i\big). \]

An offset stock then expands each face by an allowance vector \(\boldsymbol{\delta}\), giving \(\mathbf{p}_{\min}-\boldsymbol{\delta}\) and \(\mathbf{p}_{\max}+\boldsymbol{\delta}\); a general offset of an arbitrary body is a Minkowski dilation of the solid by a ball of radius \(\delta\). The AABB is orientation-dependent, which is exactly why the stock is defined in the setup's coordinate frame rather than the model's global frame.

The stock model also seeds the **in-process workpiece** (IPW), the evolving solid that results from subtracting each swept toolpath volume in sequence. The IPW is what downstream operations query to avoid recutting cleared regions (rest machining) and what a material-removal simulation displays and verifies. Because operations execute in order, the IPW couples the stock definition to the feature sequence: a roughing operation leaves an IPW that a semi-finish operation refines, and so on.

Choosing the stock type is a modeling decision with cost consequences. A box blank on a part that is mostly hollow wastes cutting time and tool life on air-heavy roughing; a from-body casting stock captures near-net shape so the plan removes only the true allowance. Turning almost always uses cylindrical stock aligned to the spindle axis. In all cases the stock must fully enclose the finished part, or the plan will attempt to produce geometry from material that was never there.
