Chamfer milling breaks a sharp edge into an angled bevel using a conical (chamfer, spot, or countersink) tool whose flank meets the workpiece at a defined angle. The path follows the edge to be broken, exactly like a contour, but the cutting element is the tool's tapered side rather than a cylindrical periphery. It is used to deburr, to remove the sharp corner left by profiling or pocketing, to prepare weld-prep bevels, and to create lead-in chamfers that ease assembly of pins, screws, and dowels.

The geometry is set by the tool's included angle and by how deep the cone is engaged along the edge. For a tool whose flank makes angle \(\theta\) with the part face (commonly \(45^\circ\)), the chamfer width \(c\) measured along the face and the vertical engagement \(z\) are linked by \(z = c\,\tan\theta\) (for \(45^\circ\), \(z = c\)). The programmed path is therefore the edge contour offset by the amount needed to bring the desired band of the cone into contact and to keep the vulnerable tool tip clear of the material, so the cone flank cuts and the point does not drag.

## Practical considerations
Because only a slanted line of the cone is in contact, cutting is light and the operation is normally a finishing pass run after the walls and top faces are complete. Consistency depends on a stable Z reference: the chamfer width is a direct function of depth, so any variation in surface height or tool stick-out changes the visible bevel. On tall edges the same bevel can be produced by different combinations of tool diameter and depth, and a larger-diameter tool run shallower keeps the contact point on a stiffer part of the cone. Corners and small internal radii limit the reachable chamfer, since the cone must physically fit the corner, and feeds are eased where the path reverses direction to protect the fine cutting edge near the tip.
