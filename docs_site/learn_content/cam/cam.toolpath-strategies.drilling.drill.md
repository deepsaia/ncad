A simple drilling cycle produces a round hole in a single continuous plunge and retract. In canonical G-code form (G81) the machine performs a fixed sequence at every hole location: rapid the tool in XY over the hole, rapid down to a retract plane (the R plane) just above the surface, feed at the programmed feed rate straight down to the final Z depth, then rapid back out. The cycle is defined precisely so that one command can be applied to a whole list of hole positions, the tool repeating the plunge-and-retract at each one.

The retract-plane concept is central. The R plane sits a small clearance above the workpiece so the tool traverses at rapid speed until it nears the material and only then switches to the cutting feed, which saves air-cutting time without risking a rapid crash into the part. After reaching depth the tool retracts either to the R plane (for the next nearby hole) or all the way to the initial plane (to clear clamps or fixtures between holes); the controller distinguishes these two retract behaviors as separate modes.

## When a plain plunge suffices
G81 has no dwell and no pecking: the drill stays engaged over the full depth, so the chips must spiral up the flutes in one continuous evacuation. This works well for shallow holes, conventionally up to roughly three diameters deep, and for the initial spot or center drilling that guides a longer drill. Beyond that ratio the flutes pack with chips, heat builds, and the drill can seize or break, which is why deeper holes switch to a pecking cycle. The relevant tool geometry is the point angle (commonly \(118^\circ\) for general work, \(135^\circ\) split points for harder or tougher materials), which sets the thrust and how the drill self-centers.
