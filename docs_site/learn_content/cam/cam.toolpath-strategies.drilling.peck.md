Peck drilling makes a deep hole in stages, interrupting the plunge to clear chips before continuing. In canonical form (G83) the tool feeds down by an increment, retracts to break and evacuate chips, then feeds back to just above the previous bottom and advances another increment, repeating until final depth. The full-retract variant pulls the drill all the way out to the retract plane on every peck so chips fall away and coolant re-floods the hole; a lighter chip-break variant (often G73) retracts only a small amount to snap the chip without fully withdrawing, trading evacuation for cycle time.

The reason pecking exists is that a continuously engaged drill relies on its flutes to spiral chips out, and that transport fails as depth grows. Once the hole is deeper than roughly three to five diameters, chips pack in the flutes, friction and heat rise sharply, coolant cannot reach the tip, and the drill work-hardens the bottom or seizes and breaks. Retracting periodically empties the flutes and restores cooling and lubrication, which is what makes deep holes reliable.

## Peck scheduling
The peck increment is the depth taken per cycle; in the canonical cycle it is given as a delta value \(q\). A common refinement is a decreasing peck schedule, where each successive peck is shorter than the last, because chip evacuation gets harder the deeper the tool is and a constant increment would over-load the later pecks. Choosing the increment trades productivity against safety: larger pecks and small retracts are faster but risk packing, while smaller pecks and full retracts are slower but far more robust in gummy or long-chipping materials. The depth-to-diameter ratio, the material's chip form, and coolant delivery (flood versus through-tool) together set how aggressive the schedule can be.
