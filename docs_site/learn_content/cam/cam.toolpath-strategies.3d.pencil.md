Pencil (or corner) finishing is a supplementary 3D strategy that machines only the **concave interior corners and fillets** of a surface where a previous finishing pass left uncut material because the ball-nose tool was too large to reach the bottom of the blend. Rather than reprocessing the whole surface, it produces a narrow path that traces the valley lines of the model, cleaning out the fillet radii and re-entrant edges in a single or a few adjacent passes.

The governing idea is a **two-point contact condition**. As the ball of radius \(r\) is dragged along an internal corner, there is a locus of tool-center positions where the ball simultaneously touches both flanking surfaces. That locus, projected onto the part, is the pencil curve. Equivalently, it is the set of points where the tool would contact the surface at two distinct points at once; between them, a smaller radius of curvature than the tool cannot be reproduced, so material remains. Formally, if a fillet has radius \(\rho\) and \(\rho < r\), the finishing tool bridges the corner and leaves a residual triangle whose depth is roughly \(r - \rho\) at the throat; the pencil path with a smaller tool is what removes it.

The path is typically generated by tracing this contact locus and offsetting it into one or a few parallel runs ("multi-pencil" or corner-rest) so the small tool blends smoothly into the surrounding finished surface rather than leaving a step. Because the engagement is deep and one-sided in a corner, feeds are reduced and the strategy is almost always run with a tool smaller than the corner radius; running pencil with a tool equal to the corner radius produces a single line of full-radius contact and is the reference case.

## Where it matters

Pencil finishing is essential in mold and die work, turbine and impeller roots, and any part with sharp internal blends, because the alternative is either a global finishing pass with an impractically small tool or leaving unswept stock. It is the natural partner of waterline and constant-scallop finishing: those cover the open faces efficiently with a larger tool, and pencil recovers only the corners the large tool could not reach. Combined with **rest machining** it forms a size-descending sequence where each smaller tool touches only what the previous one left, minimizing air cutting and total cycle time.
