Boring and reaming are finishing operations that improve a hole that already exists (usually drilled), each with a different capability. Boring uses a single-point tool mounted on a boring bar or head whose cutting radius is adjustable. Because it takes one point of contact and its diameter is set independently of any pilot hole, boring can correct the hole's location, straighten and round it, and hit a precise diameter and taper; it is the operation that trues a bore to a tight positional and geometric tolerance. The diameter is dialed in by the tool's radial offset, and finish depends on feed and nose radius just as in turning.

Reaming uses a multi-flute, fixed-diameter tool that lightly enlarges an existing hole to a close size and fine finish. It removes only a small stock allowance (commonly a few tenths of a millimeter), runs at higher feed and lower speed than the drill that preceded it, and produces a smooth, accurately sized cylindrical hole. Its key limitation is that a reamer follows the existing hole: with several teeth guiding on the bore wall it tends to track the drilled axis and will not correct positional error the way a single-point bore does. Reaming refines size and finish; it does not relocate the hole.

## Cycles and surface protection
Both are supported by canned cycles that add finishing behavior on top of the basic plunge-feed-retract. Feed-out cycles feed the tool back out at cutting feed to preserve finish rather than dragging on rapid retract. For fine bores an oriented-spindle-stop cycle stops the spindle at a known angle and shifts the tool slightly off the wall before retracting, so the cutting edge does not scratch the freshly bored surface on the way out. The practical division of labor is clear: drill to open the hole, bore when location, roundness, or an odd size must be achieved, and ream when a standard diameter needs only a better finish and tolerance while inheriting the drilled position.
