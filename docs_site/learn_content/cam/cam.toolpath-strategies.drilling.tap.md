Tapping cuts an internal thread by driving a tap into a prepared hole so its cutting teeth trace a helix at the thread's pitch. The defining constraint is that axial feed and spindle rotation are locked to the pitch: the tool must advance exactly one pitch \(P\) per revolution, so the feed rate is
\[ v_f = P \cdot n, \]
with \(n\) the spindle speed. At the bottom of the thread the spindle stops and reverses to unscrew the tap back out. In canonical form a right-hand thread uses the G84 cycle and a left-hand thread uses G74, both of which encapsulate the feed-in, stop, reverse, and feed-out motion.

Rigid tapping synchronizes spindle rotation to the Z axis electronically using spindle position feedback (an encoder), so the machine itself guarantees the pitch relationship and can decelerate, stop, and reverse the spindle in exact registration with the axis. This allows higher speeds, holding the tap in an ordinary rigid holder, and repeatable thread depth in blind holes. Its demand is on the control and drives, which must track the pitch and reverse without lag, since any mismatch between commanded feed and true thread advance is forced into the tap and the workpiece.

## Floating compensation
Where a machine cannot rigidly synchronize, or to absorb the small residual mismatch even when it can, the tap is held in a tension-compression (floating) holder. The holder lets the tap move axially a short distance relative to the spindle, so the thread itself pulls the tap at its natural pitch and the holder takes up the difference between that pitch feed and the slightly imperfect commanded feed. This protects thread form and tap life at the cost of some axial slop, which limits precision of blind-hole depth. In both approaches the pre-tap drill diameter sets the percentage of thread engaged, a higher percentage giving stronger threads but sharply higher tapping torque and breakage risk, so the tap-drill size is chosen to balance strength against the load the tap can survive.
