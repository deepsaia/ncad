Flat-pattern and profile export is the step that turns three-dimensional model information into the two-dimensional vector geometry a cutting machine can actually consume. Downstream fabrication equipment — laser, plasma, waterjet, turret punch, router, and knife cutters — operates in the plane of the stock. It needs closed contours and analytic curves, not solids. A neutral vector exchange format bridges the two worlds, and the Drawing Exchange Format (DXF) is the de facto lingua franca for this handoff because its entity set (LINE, ARC, CIRCLE, LWPOLYLINE, ELLIPSE, and SPLINE) maps cleanly onto what a motion controller can follow, and because its layer mechanism can carry cut/engrave/scribe/annotation semantics without extra metadata.

Two distinct operations hide under this heading. *Profile export* projects or slices selected faces and edges of a part onto a plane and writes the resulting outline as closed loops: the silhouette of a plate part, the outline of a machined pocket, or the boundary of a face to be cut. *Flat-pattern export* is more involved: it takes a folded sheet-metal body and **develops** (unfolds) it into the single flat blank that, once cut and bent, reproduces the 3D part. The flat pattern must therefore encode not only the outer profile and internal holes but also the bend lines and, usually on separate layers, bend-direction and bend-angle annotations so the press brake operator and the CAM system can recover the fold sequence.

## The unfolding math

Developing a bent flange is not a simple sum of leg lengths, because the material on the outside of a bend stretches while the inside compresses. Somewhere between them lies the **neutral fiber**, whose length is conserved through bending; the developed length is the sum of the flat flange lengths plus the arc length of the neutral fiber through each bend. The neutral-fiber position is parameterized by the dimensionless **K-factor** \( K \in [0, 0.5] \), the fraction of the material thickness at which strain is zero. The arc contribution, the **bend allowance** \( BA \), for a bend of interior angle \( \theta \) (degrees), inside radius \( R \), and thickness \( t \), is

\[ BA = \frac{\pi}{180}\,\theta\,\bigl(R + K\,t\bigr). \]

In practice tables are often expressed as a **bend deduction** \( BD \), the amount by which the sum of the two outside flange dimensions overshoots the true flat length. With the outside setback \( OSSB = (R+t)\tan(\theta/2) \), the two are related by \( BD = 2\,OSSB - BA \). The developed blank length across one bend is then \( L_\text{flat} = a + b - BD \), where \(a\) and \(b\) are the outside-dimension flange legs. Because \(K\) depends on the tooling, ratio \(R/t\), and material, professional workflows drive it from a per-material bend table rather than a single global constant, which is what keeps the exported blank matched to the shop that will actually form it.

## Contour fidelity and cutter-readiness

Exporting geometry cleanly is a correctness problem, not a cosmetic one. Analytic edges (lines and circular arcs) should be written as native DXF ARC/LINE/CIRCLE entities so the controller can drive them exactly; free-form NURBS edges are either emitted as SPLINE entities (degree, knot vector, and control points) or, for controllers that reject splines, approximated by arc/polyline chains to a stated **chord tolerance** — the maximum deviation between the true curve and its facets. That tolerance must be tighter than the machine's positioning tolerance yet loose enough to avoid thousands of micro-segments that stall a controller. Loops must be genuinely **closed**: tiny gaps from surface trimming have to be healed, because an open contour has no inside and cannot be offset for kerf. Convention also assigns orientation (counter-clockwise for the outer boundary, clockwise for internal holes) so that fill/void is unambiguous, and the file's unit code (\$INSUNITS) or an explicit unit agreement prevents the classic inch-versus-millimetre scaling disaster on the shop floor.

Getting this stage right matters because everything downstream inherits its errors. Kerf compensation, lead-in/lead-out placement, micro-joints or bridges that keep parts from dropping, and nesting all assume clean, closed, correctly oriented contours at the right scale. A robust exporter therefore separates concerns onto layers (one for the cut profile, one for etch/scribe, one for bend lines, one for text) and documents its chord tolerance and unit handling, so the same blank produced today is byte-for-byte reproducible tomorrow and portable across cutting platforms.
