On a lathe (turning center) the **workpiece rotates** about the spindle axis while a single-point tool translates to remove material, the mirror image of milling, where the tool spins and the part is still. This kinematics is ideal for parts with rotational symmetry: shafts, bushings, threads, and bores are generated by moving the tool in a plane containing the spindle axis. The two primary slides are Z (along the spindle centerline) and X (radially, controlling diameter); facing, turning, boring, grooving, and parting are all combinations of motion in this Z-X plane.

## Cutting speed and constant surface speed

The surface speed at the cutting point depends on the local diameter,

\[ v = \pi D N, \]

where \(D\) is the work diameter at the tool and \(N\) the spindle speed. On a facing cut \(D\) shrinks toward the center, so at fixed \(N\) the surface speed would fall to zero. **Constant surface speed** control fixes \(v\) instead and continuously commands

\[ N = \frac{v}{\pi D}, \]

raising spindle speed as the tool moves inward, subject to a maximum-rpm clamp near the center. Holding \(v\) constant keeps finish and tool wear uniform across a face, which is why turning controllers expose an explicit constant-surface-speed mode alongside a fixed-rpm mode.

**Threading** is the most kinematically demanding turning operation: the tool must advance exactly one thread lead per spindle revolution, over multiple passes, all starting at the same angular point. The controller therefore **synchronizes feed to spindle rotation**, reading a spindle encoder so that \(v_f = (\text{lead}) \times N\) and every pass indexes to the same start angle. A dedicated feed rate detached from the spindle would let the thread "drift" and destroy the previously cut groove.

Turning centers routinely add capabilities that extend this base kinematics: a tailstock or steady rest to support long slender work against deflection, a driven tool and C-axis for light milling (the boundary into mill-turn), and a sub-spindle for back-side work. The defining strength of turning is the speed and finish it achieves on round features from bar or chuck stock; its limitation is that genuinely non-rotational geometry needs those added axes or a separate milling setup.
