Workholding is the art of holding a part in a **repeatable, rigid, accessible** position while it is machined. A fixture must do two things that are in tension: fully constrain the part so it does not move under cutting forces, yet leave the machined surfaces open to the tool. Vises and clamps grip prismatic stock, chucks and collets grip round stock for turning, and dedicated fixtures locate a specific family of parts on precision pads and pins. Poor workholding is a leading cause of scrap: if the part shifts, springs, or seats differently between runs, no amount of machine accuracy recovers the tolerance.

## The 3-2-1 locating principle

A rigid body has six degrees of freedom (three translations, three rotations). The classic **3-2-1 principle** removes them with a minimal, deterministic set of locators: three points on the **primary** datum face fix one translation and two rotations, two points on the **secondary** face fix a second translation and the last rotation, and one point on the **tertiary** face fixes the final translation. Six locating points thus constrain all six freedoms without over-constraint, giving a single, repeatable seated position.

## Locating versus clamping: form and force closure

Locators establish position; **clamps** hold the part against those locators. It is essential that clamps push the part *into* the locators, not fight them, so the locating scheme stays deterministic. Two ways to immobilize a part are distinguished: **form closure**, where the geometry of contacts alone prevents any motion (as with a full enclosure or interference), and **force closure**, where friction from applied clamping force resists motion. Most practical fixtures rely on force closure, so the clamp must supply enough normal force \(F_n\) that friction \(\mu F_n\) exceeds the cutting force tending to slide or lift the part, while staying low enough not to distort a thin wall or mark a finished surface.

Because clamping force, cutting force, and part stiffness interact, fixture design is an optimization, not a lookup: place locators on stiff, already-finished datums; apply clamps opposite locators and over supported regions; and verify that the resultant of all cutting forces stays within the friction cone at every contact throughout the cut. Chucks apply the same logic radially, trading jaw grip force against the risk of crushing thin-walled rings or leaving three-lobe distortion from three-jaw contact.
