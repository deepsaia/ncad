Pocketing clears all material inside a closed boundary down to a flat floor, producing an enclosed cavity. Because the walls are vertical and the floor is horizontal, it is a classic 2.5D operation: motion in the XY plane at a fixed Z, repeated at stepped depths. Pockets may contain islands (regions left standing), and the toolpath must respect both the outer boundary and each island offset by the tool radius so the finished walls land exactly on the part geometry.

Two path families dominate. Direction-parallel (raster or zig-zag) paths sweep back and forth across the region; they are simple but produce many entries into the wall and inconsistent up/down-milling. Contour-parallel paths, the concentric-offset strategy, are generated by repeatedly offsetting the boundary inward by the radial stepover \(a_e\) until the region is exhausted. This keeps a consistent climb or conventional engagement and follows the part shape, but leaves uncut pockets of material near the medial axis (the skeleton where offsets from opposite walls meet), which must be detected and cleaned up. The offsetting and uncut-region analysis are fundamentally computational-geometry problems on the boundary's medial axis or Voronoi diagram.

## Depth stepping and entry
Pocketing removes a 3D volume by stacking 2D layers, each advancing the depth by the axial step \(a_p\) until the floor is reached. Because a flat end mill cuts poorly when plunged straight down (its center has zero cutting speed), each level must be entered with a helical or linear ramp, or a pre-drilled plunge hole. The offset spacing obeys \(a_e < R\) at the tool radius to avoid leaving a ridge, and narrow necks in the pocket can force full-width (slotting) engagement that must be recognized and fed conservatively.

The practical challenge is maintaining a bounded, near-constant engagement angle. Sharp internal corners cause the tool to wrap and the engagement to spike, which spikes cutting force and can break the tool; smoothing corners with arcs, or switching to trochoidal (looping) motion, keeps the load steady. A typical recipe is a roughing pocket that leaves a thin stock allowance on walls and floor, followed by a separate finishing contour pass that takes that allowance in one light cut for accuracy and finish.
