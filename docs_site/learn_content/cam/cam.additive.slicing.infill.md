Infill is the internal lattice that fills the volume enclosed by a part's perimeters. Solid material is rarely needed throughout the interior: for most parts, a sparse periodic structure supplies enough stiffness, strength, and support for the top surfaces while dramatically cutting material use and build time. The key parameter is *relative density* (infill density) \(\bar\rho = \rho^{*}/\rho_s\), the ratio of the cellular structure's apparent density to that of the fully dense base material, usually expressed as a percentage of the enclosed area (or volume) occupied by deposited material. Practical values range from a few percent for lightweight, non-structural volume to fully dense for load-bearing regions.

## Why density governs properties

Infill behaves as an engineered cellular solid, so its effective mechanical properties follow the scaling laws of cellular materials rather than those of the bulk polymer or metal. To first order the relative modulus and relative strength obey power laws in relative density,

\[ \frac{E^{*}}{E_s} = C\left(\frac{\rho^{*}}{\rho_s}\right)^{n}, \qquad \frac{\sigma^{*}}{\sigma_s} = C'\left(\frac{\rho^{*}}{\rho_s}\right)^{m}, \]

where the exponent depends on the deformation mechanism of the unit cell. *Bending-dominated* topologies (open, randomly connected cells) give \(n \approx 2\), so halving density quarters the stiffness. *Stretch-dominated* topologies, whose members are aligned to carry axial load, give \(n \approx 1\) and are far more efficient per unit mass. This is the mechanical reason different infill patterns exist: they are simply different unit cells trading isotropy, efficiency, and printability.

## Pattern families

Infill patterns fall into a few families. *Linear and rectilinear* patterns (parallel lines, grids, triangles) are fast and mechanically anisotropic, strong along the deposition axes and weak transverse. *Cellular tilings* such as honeycomb and its variants approximate stretch-dominated behavior in-plane and give a high stiffness-to-weight ratio. *Space-filling and self-avoiding curves* (for example, a single continuous serpentine or Hilbert-type path) minimize travel moves and retraction, which matters for extrusion-based processes. *Triply periodic minimal surfaces* such as the gyroid produce smooth, matrix-connected, near-isotropic cells with no sharp stress concentrations and good behavior under multi-axial loading. The choice couples geometry to the physics of the process: patterns must be manufacturable at the chosen bead width and must self-support across layer transitions.

Beyond a single global value, slicers support *graded* and *variable* infill, where \(\bar\rho\) is a spatial field \(\bar\rho(\mathbf{x})\) tuned to the local stress state, so material is placed where load paths demand it and removed elsewhere. This connects infill directly to topology and lattice optimization. Infill also serves non-structural roles: it supports the *solid top layers* (the sparse lattice must span a bead-width gap without excessive sag, which sets a floor on density under flat roofs), controls thermal mass and warping, and in some applications tunes acoustic or energy-absorption response. Because the pattern is periodic while the part boundary is arbitrary, the slicer must clip the infill lattice to the inner perimeter and reconcile the two, typically leaving a small overlap so the infill fuses to the walls.
