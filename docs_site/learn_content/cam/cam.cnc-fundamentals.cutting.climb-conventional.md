**Climb** and **conventional** milling name the two ways a rotating cutter can be fed relative to the workpiece, and the distinction is defined by how tooth rotation relates to feed direction at the point of contact. In **conventional (up) milling** the cutter teeth move *against* the feed direction where they meet the material, so each tooth enters at zero chip thickness and exits at maximum: the chip is thin-to-thick. In **climb (down) milling** the teeth move *with* the feed at entry, so each tooth bites at maximum chip thickness and exits at zero: thick-to-thin.

The chip-thickness order has direct mechanical consequences. In conventional milling the edge must **rub and dwell** at the start of each cut before the chip thickness exceeds the minimum needed to shear, which generates heat, work-hardens the surface ahead of the cut, and increases flank wear; the cutting force also tends to lift the workpiece off the table. In climb milling the tooth engages a full chip immediately and then unloads, which reduces rubbing, runs cooler, generally gives better surface finish and longer tool life, and pushes the workpiece down into the fixture and the cutting force into the feed drive.

The historical reason conventional milling persisted is **backlash**. On a machine with lash in the feed screw, climb milling is dangerous on the entry side: because the cutting force acts in the feed direction, it can snatch the table forward across the backlash gap, suddenly increasing engagement and breaking the tool or gouging the part. Manual machines with worn screws were therefore run conventionally by rule. Modern machines with preloaded ballscrews or backlash compensation eliminate this hazard, which is why climb milling is the default on numerically controlled equipment.

Because the choice affects finish and force direction, it is a first-class toolpath parameter, and a single strategy often *mixes* the two. On a closed profile, one side is climb and the other conventional unless the path is arranged to keep the same side of the tool engaged. Finishing passes are typically all-climb for surface quality; some hardened or abrasive materials, or cases where a hard skin (as on castings) sits on top, may favor conventional entry so the edge starts beneath the scale rather than dragging across it. The competent plan sets cut direction deliberately per operation rather than accepting whatever the geometry produces.
