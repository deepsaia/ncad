Most NC languages are **modal**: once a mode is commanded it persists across subsequent blocks until explicitly changed. Modality exists to keep programs short and to match how a machinist thinks ("keep feeding in a straight line until I say otherwise"). Modes are organized into *modal groups*, sets of mutually exclusive states, only one of which can be active at a time. The motion group (\(G0, G1, G2, G3\), the canned cycles, ...), the plane group (\(G17/G18/G19\)), the distance group (\(G90/G91\)), the units group (\(G20/G21\)), and the feed-rate-mode group are all examples. Because a bare `X Y` block inherits the current motion mode, a post-processor must track modal state precisely to know whether that block will rapid or cut.

**Canned cycles** are prepackaged, parameterized motion macros for recurring operations, most importantly drilling, peck drilling, tapping, and boring. Instead of emitting the four or five moves of every hole (rapid to the hole, rapid to a clearance plane, feed to depth, dwell, retract), the program issues a single cycle word and the controller expands it internally at each commanded point. A drilling cycle \(G81\), for instance, is defined by the retract plane, the final \(Z\) depth, and the feed rate; peck drilling \(G83\) adds an incremental peck depth \(Q\) so the tool repeatedly withdraws to clear chips; tapping \(G84\) synchronizes feed to spindle so the pitch is honored. A representative block is `G83 X.. Y.. Z-1.0 R0.1 Q0.25 F8`.

## Retract mode and the R plane

Canned cycles interact with two other modal states that a post must manage carefully. The retract-mode group (\(G98/G99\)) selects whether the tool returns to the *initial* level or only to the *R* (clearance) plane between holes, the difference determines whether the tool clears clamps and fixtures on the way to the next point. Distance mode also changes the meaning of the cycle parameters: in incremental mode the \(R\) and \(Z\) values are measured relative to the current position rather than the work origin, a frequent source of crashes if mishandled. The canonical sequence executed by a single \(G81\) point can be summarized as
\[
\text{rapid to }(X,Y)\;\rightarrow\;\text{rapid to }R\;\rightarrow\;\text{feed to }Z\;\rightarrow\;\text{retract to }R\ \text{or initial}.
\]

Canned cycles are themselves modal: once \(G81\) is active, each following block that supplies only \(X\,Y\) triggers another full cycle at the new point, and the cycle stays active until cancelled with \(G80\). This is powerful and dangerous in equal measure, an unexpected \(X\,Y\) after a forgotten \(G80\) will plunge a drilling cycle where a simple positioning move was intended.

The engineering reason canned cycles and modality exist is real-time efficiency and determinism. Controllers historically had tight memory and slow serial links; expanding a hundred holes into a five-word cycle plus a hundred coordinate pairs, rather than five hundred explicit blocks, was essential. For a post-processor the obligation is twofold: emit cycles where the target controller supports them (some dialects lack certain cycles and require long-hand expansion), and never lose track of modal state, because the geometric meaning of every subsequent block depends on the modes left standing.
