Forming reshapes material by **plastic deformation** rather than by removing or adding it: the workpiece is forced past its yield point so that it takes a new permanent shape while conserving volume. Because almost no material becomes scrap and the process can run in seconds, forming underpins high-volume manufacturing. The family divides broadly into **sheet forming** (bending, deep drawing, stamping), where a thin blank is shaped mostly in-plane and through-thickness stress is small, and **bulk forming** (forging, rolling, extrusion), where all three principal dimensions change substantially. Casting is often grouped here as a net-shape route, though it solidifies a melt in a mold rather than deforming a solid.

## Plastic flow, hardening, and volume constancy

Deformation is measured with **true (logarithmic) strain**, \(\varepsilon = \ln(\ell/\ell_0)\), because it adds correctly over sequential steps and respects the incompressibility of plastic flow:

\[ \varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2 + \varepsilon_3 = 0. \]

Most metals **strain-harden**, so the flow stress rises as the material is worked. A widely used power-law (Hollomon) description is

\[ \sigma = K\, \varepsilon^{\,n}, \]

where \(K\) is the strength coefficient and \(n\) the strain-hardening exponent. The exponent \(n\) also predicts formability: it equals the true strain at the onset of necking in a tension test, so higher \(n\) means the material can stretch further before it localizes and tears. Forming loads follow directly, since the press force must supply the flow stress over the contact area plus friction and redundant-work penalties.

Two effects dominate the geometry a former can actually achieve. **Springback**: because part of the deformation is elastic, the part relaxes toward its original shape when the tool releases, so bends are overbent and dies are compensated to hit the target angle. **Forming limits**: every combination of major and minor surface strain has a boundary (the forming-limit diagram) beyond which the sheet necks or splits, which constrains how sharp a corner or how deep a draw can be made in one operation.

Forming matters wherever part count is large and material efficiency and speed dominate cost: automotive panels, cans, fasteners, and forged structural components whose worked grain flow gives superior fatigue strength. Its weaknesses are the high cost and lead time of dedicated tooling (dies must be designed for springback and wear), limited feature complexity per operation, and residual stresses that may require a stress-relief step.
