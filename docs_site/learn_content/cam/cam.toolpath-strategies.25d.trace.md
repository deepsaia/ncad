Tracing (engraving) drives a tool directly along a curve or centerline, with no tool-radius offset, to inscribe marks into a surface: part numbers, serials, logos, scales, or decorative text. Because the tool follows the geometry itself rather than an offset of it, the toolpath is the source curve projected to a shallow depth. The operation is about controlled, legible removal rather than bulk material clearing, so depth and feed are small and consistency of depth is the primary quality metric.

The tool of choice is usually a conical engraving cutter (V-bit) or a very small end mill. With a V-bit the engraved line width is coupled to depth: for a tool with included angle \(2\alpha\), a plunge of depth \(d\) produces a groove of width
\[ w = 2\,d\,\tan\alpha. \]
This coupling is exploited in V-carving, where the controller varies depth along the path so the line swells and tapers, reproducing the calligraphic stroke weight of a font by driving the tool deeper on wide strokes and shallower into thin serifs and corners. A constant-width scribe, by contrast, holds depth fixed and simply follows the centerline.

## Depth control and reference
Because the visible result depends on holding a few tenths of a millimeter of depth over a possibly non-flat surface, tracing is sensitive to workpiece flatness and Z reference. Precise touch-off, a rigid setup, and sometimes a spring-loaded or floating nose that rides the surface are used to keep depth uniform. When the mark must sit on the geometry line exactly (a fiducial or a scribe used for downstream alignment), no offset is applied and the tool centerline is the target; when a channel of defined width is wanted, the path may instead be treated as a narrow contour. Feeds are moderated at sharp corners so the small, fragile tool is not overloaded where direction reverses.
