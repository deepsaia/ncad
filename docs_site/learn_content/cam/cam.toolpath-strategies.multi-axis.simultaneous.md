Simultaneous five-axis machining moves all five machine axes, three linear and two rotary, **together and continuously** so that the tool's orientation varies along the path while the contact point tracks the surface. Unlike positional 3+2, where the tool axis is fixed per operation, here the tool axis \(\mathbf{t}(u)\) is a function of path parameter, letting the cutter maintain an ideal attitude relative to a curved surface at every instant. It is the strategy behind swarf (flank) milling, flow-line surface finishing, and general multiaxis contouring of impellers, blades, and undercut geometry.

The defining problem is **tool-orientation control under constraints**. At each point the axis must satisfy the surface tangency or flank-contact condition while avoiding local gouging (the tool cutting into the surface it should just touch), rear/heel gouging, and global collisions of holder and machine with the part. In swarf milling the side of a (possibly tapered) tool is kept tangent to a ruled or near-ruled surface, so the tool axis is aligned with the surface's rulings; in flow-line finishing the axis is tilted by a lead/tilt pair \((\alpha,\beta)\) from the surface normal to control the effective cutting radius and avoid the zero-speed center of a ball nose. Solving for a smooth, gouge-free \(\mathbf{t}(u)\) is a coupled geometric and kinematic optimization with no closed form in general.

The kinematics also do not decouple: converting the desired tip position and axis into machine axis commands requires the machine's **inverse kinematics**, and rotary motion couples into linear motion. A small rotation near a **kinematic singularity** (for a table-tilt machine, when the tool aligns with the primary rotary axis) demands enormous, near-infinite rotary velocity to keep the tip on path, so path planning must detect and route around singularities and enforce rotary velocity/acceleration limits. This is why post-processing and machine-specific verification are inseparable from the toolpath itself.

## Why it is a specialized capability

All of the above, continuous gouge-free orientation, ruled-surface flank contact, singularity avoidance, full-assembly collision checking, and machine-specific inverse kinematics, make simultaneous five-axis a fundamentally harder class of problem than three-axis or 3+2 work, and it is typically handled by a dedicated geometry kernel rather than as an extension of planar strategies. Its payoff is equally large: a single flank pass can finish a ruled wall that would otherwise need thousands of ball-nose scallop passes, and continuous tool tilt reaches undercuts and keeps a favorable cutting speed on shallow surfaces where a fixed-axis ball nose would rub at its dead center. For that reason it is commonly scoped as a separate, external-kernel capability rather than part of a general three-axis toolpath engine.
