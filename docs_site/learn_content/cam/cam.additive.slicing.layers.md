Layer slicing is the process-planning step that converts a three-dimensional solid into the stack of thin, planar cross-sections an additive machine actually builds. Every material-addition process fabricates a part one layer at a time along a build direction (conventionally \(+z\)); slicing intersects the part geometry with a family of horizontal planes \(z = z_0 + k\,h\) for integer layer index \(k\) and layer thickness \(h\). Each intersection produces one or more closed planar contours that bound the solid material at that height. Because the source geometry is usually a tessellated boundary representation (a triangle mesh) or a NURBS B-rep, the slicer must compute plane-triangle or plane-surface intersections robustly, stitch the resulting edge segments into oriented loops, and resolve nesting so that inner loops correctly represent holes via the even-odd or nonzero winding rule.

## The staircase effect and cusp height

Discretizing a curved or inclined surface into finite layers introduces an unavoidable geometric error known as the staircase effect. The controlling quantity is the *cusp height* \(c\): the maximum deviation between the stepped layer boundary and the ideal surface. For a surface whose outward normal makes angle \(\theta\) with the build direction,

\[ c = h\,\lvert\cos\theta\rvert. \]

Vertical walls (\(\theta = 90^\circ\)) incur no staircase error, while shallow, near-horizontal surfaces (\(\theta \to 0\)) suffer the worst, with \(c \to h\). This is why uniform slicing forces a blunt trade-off: thin layers everywhere give good surface fidelity but long build times, thick layers do the reverse.

## Adaptive slicing and perimeters

Adaptive slicing removes that compromise by varying \(h\) locally. Inverting the cusp relation gives the largest layer thickness that still respects a tolerance \(c_{max}\): \( h = c_{max}/\lvert\cos\theta\rvert \), clamped to the machine's minimum and maximum layer bounds. Regions of steep, flat-topped geometry are sliced thin; near-vertical regions are sliced thick. This idea was formalized in the early slicing literature and remains the basis of modern variable-height planning.

Once a layer contour exists, it does not directly become the deposition path, because the deposited bead (or fused track) has finite width \(w\). The slicer offsets the contour inward by \(w/2\) to place the *first perimeter* (also called the outer shell or wall) so the outside of the bead lands on the true part boundary. Additional perimeters are generated by successive inward offsets of \(w\), building a multi-wall shell whose thickness is an integer multiple of the bead width. Contour offsetting is nontrivial: inward offsets can cause loops to self-intersect, split, or vanish in thin ribs, so slicers use polygon-offset algorithms (Minkowski erosion, straight-skeleton, or medial-axis methods) and detect gaps too narrow for a full bead, which are filled with reduced-width or gap-fill traces. The perimeters largely determine visible surface quality and dimensional accuracy, while the enclosed region is later subdivided into solid top/bottom layers and sparse infill.
