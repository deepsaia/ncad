Stock-removal simulation reconstructs the shape a workpiece acquires as a cutting tool sweeps through it, so that the programmed toolpath can be verified geometrically before a single chip is cut. The core abstraction is a **regularized Boolean difference**: starting from a solid model of the raw stock \(S_0\), each motion of the tool removes the volume the tool occupies as it travels. If \(V_i\) is the *swept volume* generated by the tool along motion segment \(i\) (the union of every instantaneous tool position between the start and end of that move), the in-process workpiece evolves as

\[
S_i \;=\; S_{i-1} \;\setminus^{*}\; V_i,
\]

where \(\setminus^{*}\) denotes the *regularized* set difference: the ordinary set difference followed by taking the closure of the interior, so the result stays a well-formed solid without dangling faces, cracks, or infinitely thin walls. After \(n\) moves the final simulated part is \(S_n = S_0 \setminus^{*} \bigcup_i V_i\).

## Why it exists

A numeric toolpath is just a stream of positions and feeds; nothing in it guarantees the resulting geometry matches intent. Boolean stock simulation turns that path back into a solid you can measure. Because the output is a genuine solid, its **mass properties** are well defined, and the difference between two states gives the material removed by any operation or by the whole program:

\[
\Delta V = \mathrm{vol}(S_{i-1}) - \mathrm{vol}(S_i), \qquad \Delta m = \rho\,\Delta V .
\]

This feeds directly into a bill of materials and cost estimate: raw stock mass, finished part mass, scrap mass, and the swarf produced per operation all fall out of the volume deltas, and the removed-volume-per-time also underpins material-removal-rate estimates used for cycle-time and tool-load checks.

## Representations and their trade-offs

The governing idea is representation-independent, but the data structure chosen for \(S_i\) dictates accuracy and speed. An **exact boundary representation (B-rep)** performs true surface/surface intersection for each Boolean, yielding watertight NURBS or analytic faces and exact mass properties, but robustly intersecting thousands of swept volumes is numerically delicate and slow. **Discretized representations** trade exactness for speed and robustness: voxel grids approximate the solid as occupied cells; **dexel** (depth-element) and **z-map** structures store, along a regular ray grid, the entry/exit depths of material, so a Boolean becomes a fast interval subtraction per ray; triple-dexel (rays along all three axes) recovers near-isotropic accuracy. Discretized methods make real-time visualization tractable and never fail to produce *a* result, at the cost of resolution-limited volumes and surfaces.

## Where it matters

Stock-removal simulation is the foundation for every downstream verification: the simulated in-process stock is the reference against which excess material and gouges are measured, and it is the moving body a collision check tests against fixtures and holders. In practice the Boolean state is also carried between setups so that a second operation begins from the true as-machined stock rather than the original block, which is essential for rest-machining and for accurate mass and cost accounting across a multi-setup process plan.
