A **machining feature** is a region of a part carrying manufacturing semantics rather than pure geometry: a hole, a pocket, a slot, a step, a boss, a chamfer, a face. Each feature type implies a family of operations, tools, and access directions. Feature recognition and selection is the bridge between the design model (a boundary representation of faces, edges, and vertices) and the process plan (an ordered sequence of operations). It answers the question *what is there to machine, and how should it be approached?*

There are two broad routes. **Interactive selection** lets a planner pick faces and edges directly and label them as the boundary of a feature; this is robust but manual. **Automatic feature recognition** (AFR) infers features from the solid model without human labeling. Classical AFR techniques include graph-based methods that match subgraphs of the face-adjacency graph (edges labeled convex or concave) against a library of feature patterns; **hint-based** methods that treat certain topological or geometric traces as evidence for a feature and then complete it volumetrically; and **volumetric decomposition** methods that split the removal volume (stock minus part) into machinable cells. Each has known strengths and failure modes, especially where features intersect and destroy the tell-tale topology, which is precisely why interacting-feature recognition remains hard.

The difficulty is fundamental: features can **overlap and interact**, one feature's faces may be partly consumed by another, and a given geometry can admit multiple valid feature interpretations depending on the intended process. A slot crossing a pocket may be seen as one feature or two; a filleted corner may belong to either. There is generally no unique decomposition, so recognition is best understood as producing *plausible* interpretations to be ranked by manufacturability, not a single ground truth.

Selection carries a subtle robustness requirement: the association from an operation to its geometry must **survive rebuilds**. When the underlying model is edited, faces and edges are regenerated and their internal identifiers can change, so a selection stored as a raw index silently re-binds to the wrong entity. Persistent, name-based topological references (matching on stable characteristics such as generating feature, adjacency, and geometry rather than transient IDs) are what let a recognized feature and its operation re-select correctly after the part changes. This is the same topological-naming problem that governs parametric modeling, surfacing here because a toolpath is anchored to selected faces.

Recognized features feed directly into planning: feature type selects candidate tools and cycles (a hole feature offers drilling, boring, or helical interpolation), feature access direction constrains setup and tool orientation, and feature interactions dictate ordering (a boss must exist before the pocket around it is finished). Treating machining as a set of features, rather than an undifferentiated cloud of surfaces, is what makes automated and semi-automated process planning tractable.
