Slotting cuts a channel whose width equals the tool diameter, with the cutter fully embedded so it engages material across its entire diameter. This full-immersion condition, a radial engagement \(a_e = D\) and a tool-wrap angle of \(180^\circ\), is the most demanding milling situation because both flanks of the cutter are cutting simultaneously. There is no clean climb-versus-conventional choice: one side climbs while the other cuts conventionally, so the side forces largely cancel but the radial load and heat are maximal.

The dominant problems are chip evacuation and heat. A fully engaged cutter has no open side to eject chips, so chips are carried around and recut, packing into the flutes; this generates heat, raises torque, and can weld chips to the edge or snap the tool. Cutting temperature concentrates because there is little air or coolant reaching the fully wrapped edge. Deflection is also significant: the balanced but large radial force bends the tool, widening or bell-mouthing the slot.

## Managing full engagement
Because of these effects, a straight full-width slot is cut conservatively: reduced feed per tooth, generous through-tool coolant, and shallow axial steps so the flute length stays clear. Entry is by ramping or helical plunge rather than a vertical plunge, since an end-mill center cannot cut efficiently straight down. Where geometry allows, a more robust alternative is to open the slot with trochoidal motion: the tool follows overlapping loops narrower than the slot so radial engagement stays a small fraction of \(D\), which lets it run at high speed and depth while keeping force and heat low, then a finishing pass trues the walls to width. The material-removal rate for a straight slot is \( \dot V = a_e \, a_p \, v_f = D \, a_p \, v_f \), so any gain from full width must be weighed against the tool life and reliability cost of full immersion.
