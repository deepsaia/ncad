## Section views

Interior features that would otherwise appear as a clutter of hidden lines are shown by cutting the model with an imaginary plane, discarding the material between the plane and the viewer, and drawing what remains. Formally the cutting plane is a point \(p\) with normal \(n\); a model point \(x\) is kept when \((x-p)\cdot n \le 0\) and removed otherwise. Faces newly exposed by the cut, meaning the solid material the plane passes through, are filled with section lining (hatching); voids the plane crosses are left blank. A cutting-plane line with direction arrows and identifying letters marks where the cut is taken, and the resulting view is labelled to match, for example SECTION A-A.

Common variants trade completeness for clarity. A full section cuts entirely through the part; a half section cuts a symmetric part halfway so one half shows the interior and the other the exterior. An offset section uses a stepped plane to capture several features in one view; an aligned section rotates angled features into the plane so their true spacing reads. A broken-out (local) section removes only a small region bounded by a freehand break line, and a revolved or removed section shows the cross-section of a bar, arm, or rib. By convention, section lining is suppressed on thin ribs, webs, and standard parts such as fasteners and shafts even when the plane passes through them, because hatching them would falsely suggest solid bulk.

## Detail, broken, and crop views

A detail view enlarges a small region of a parent view at a larger scale so small features can be dimensioned; it is bounded by a circle or boundary on the parent and labelled with its own scale. A broken view removes one or more uniform middle stretches of a long, prismatic part (a shaft, beam, or extrusion) and closes the gap with break lines, so the part fits the sheet while dimensions still report its true, unbroken length. A crop (clip) view discards everything outside a user-defined boundary, trimming a view to just the area of interest without changing its scale or projection. All of these are derived, associative views: they reference the same model and rebuild when it changes, so the section, the detail, and the break stay correct as the design evolves.
