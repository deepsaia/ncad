A **datum** is a theoretically exact geometric reference — a point, axis, line, or plane — from which the location and orientation of other features are established and verified. It is an abstraction: perfectly straight, flat, or round. The physical counterpart is the **datum feature**, an actual (imperfect) surface, hole, or edge on the part that the designer nominates to establish that reference. Because the real feature is never perfect, a **datum feature simulator** (a surface plate, a gauge pin, a chuck, or their mathematical equivalent) contacts the datum feature and derives the exact datum from it. This three-part distinction — feature, simulator, datum — is the conceptual core of the whole system: tolerances are meaningless without an unambiguous frame in which to measure them.

## The datum reference frame

Most dimensioning is done against a **datum reference frame (DRF)**: three mutually perpendicular planes intersecting at an origin, which together fully constrain a rigid body's six degrees of freedom (three translations, three rotations). Datums are invoked in a strict precedence — primary, secondary, tertiary — and that order matters because it dictates how the part is seated. A planar **primary** datum contacts the simulator at a minimum of three points and removes three degrees of freedom (one translation and two rotations). The **secondary** removes two more (one translation, one rotation) on at least two points, and the **tertiary** removes the last (one translation) on one point. The 3-2-1 seating rule follows directly from this budget:

\[ \underbrace{3}_{\text{primary}} + \underbrace{2}_{\text{secondary}} + \underbrace{1}_{\text{tertiary}} = 6 \text{ degrees of freedom} \]

Swapping which surface is primary changes the seated orientation of the part and therefore changes measured results, which is why the callout order — not merely the set of datums — is part of the specification.

## Datum targets

When a feature cannot serve as a full datum — a rough casting or forging surface, a weldment, sheet metal that flexes, or any part whose contact is genuinely local — the standard allows **datum targets**: specified points, lines, or bounded areas of contact rather than the entire surface. A target is dimensioned to an exact location on the feature and identified in a divided circular symbol (upper half = target size/area, lower half = datum letter and target number, e.g. `A1`). Targets let a repeatable, minimal set of contact locations define the reference frame so that an irregular or unstable surface still yields a deterministic fixture and gauge.

Datums also interact with **material condition modifiers**. When a datum feature of size (a hole or pin) is referenced at maximum material boundary rather than as a resolved actual feature, the simulator becomes a fixed-size boundary and the part is allowed to shift within the clearance — a *datum shift* analogous to bonus tolerance. This is essential for functional gauging of assemblies of fasteners and mating parts, where what matters is that everything fits, not that every feature sits at its nominal center. Correctly chosen datums are the single most consequential decision in a tolerancing scheme: they encode the part's function (how it mounts, mates, and is inspected) and they make the geometric tolerances that reference them repeatable across shops and metrology systems.
