A *blend* is a transition surface that smooths the junction between two (or more) primary surfaces, replacing the sharp edge along their intersection with a rounded or shaped patch. The *fillet* - a constant- or variable-radius rounding of an edge - is the most common blend and one of the most heavily used operations in mechanical design, because interior fillets relieve stress concentrations and exterior fillets (rounds) remove sharp corners for safety and manufacturability.

## The rolling-ball model

The dominant construction is the *rolling-ball* (or rolling-sphere) blend. Imagine a ball of radius \(r\) rolling along the concave side of the edge while staying tangent to *both* primary surfaces at once. Three loci emerge and define the blend exactly: the ball's center traces the *spine*; the two moving points where the ball touches the surfaces trace the *contact curves* (also called linkage curves or trimlines); and the blend surface is swept out by the circular arc that spans the two contact points on each ball position. The primary surfaces are then *trimmed* back to the contact curves and the blend is stitched in between. A *variable-radius* fillet drives \(r\) as a function of position along the edge, and a *chordal* or *disc* blend fixes the section width rather than the radius.

## Continuity and where blends break

Continuity across the seams is the quality lever. A *tangent* (\(G^1\)) blend, which a plain rolling ball produces, meets each primary surface with a common tangent plane; higher-end surfacing demands *curvature* (\(G^2\)) continuity so that reflection lines and highlights flow across the seam without the visible break that a curvature jump causes. Achieving \(G^2\) generally requires a richer section profile than a circular arc (a conic or general parametric section), which is why professional systems offer conic and "smooth" fillet options in addition to circular ones.

Blends fail in predictable ways, and the failure modes echo the offset problem because a fillet is essentially an offset-and-trim operation. If the requested radius exceeds the local concave radius of curvature of either primary surface, the rolling ball cannot maintain contact and the blend self-intersects or collapses. Where several filleted edges meet at a vertex, the individual blends do not close cleanly and a *setback* or *vertex blend* patch must fill the corner. Robust blending therefore depends on reliable surface-surface intersection, careful trimming of the primaries to the contact curves, and detection of the degenerate radius/curvature conditions before geometry is committed.
