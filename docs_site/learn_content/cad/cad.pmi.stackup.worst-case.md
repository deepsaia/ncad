A **tolerance stack-up** answers a design question that no single dimension can: given that every part in an assembly is manufactured somewhere within its own tolerances, what is the range of some critical *resultant* dimension — a gap, an interference, an overhang, a wall thickness? The **worst-case** (also called *limit* or *arithmetic*) method assumes the pessimistic scenario in which every contributing feature simultaneously sits at whichever end of its tolerance band pushes the result in the same direction. It is the most conservative analysis possible, and it guarantees **100% interchangeability**: if the worst-case gap is acceptable, then *any* combination of in-tolerance parts will assemble and function.

## The governing arithmetic

Model the resultant dimension \( Y \) as a function of the contributing dimensions \( x_i \). Linearizing about the nominal, each contributor has a **sensitivity** \( a_i = \partial Y / \partial x_i \) — for a simple 1-D chain of adding and subtracting lengths, every \( a_i \) is just \( +1 \) or \( -1 \), while for angled or lever-arm geometry it is a trigonometric factor. The nominal result is the signed sum of nominal contributors, and the worst-case tolerance is the **sum of the absolute values** of the scaled contributor tolerances:

\[ Y_{\text{nom}} = \sum_i a_i\, x_{i,\text{nom}}, \qquad T_{wc} = \sum_i \lvert a_i \rvert\, t_i \]

where \( t_i \) is the equal-bilateral half-tolerance of contributor \( i \). The resultant then ranges over \( Y_{\text{nom}} \pm T_{wc} \). Building the stack is a bookkeeping exercise: walk a continuous loop or vector chain through the assembly from one side of the gap to the other, assign each dimension a direction sign, convert any unequal or geometric tolerances (position zones, runout, profile) into equivalent \( \pm \) contributions, and sum. Careful analysts include the *effects* of geometric tolerances — a position tolerance contributes its radial value, and material-condition bonus can widen or, if you seek the tightest fit, must be considered at its least favorable value.

## When worst-case is (and isn't) the right tool

Because \( T_{wc} \) grows linearly with the number of contributors, long chains force each individual tolerance to be very tight to keep the resultant in bounds — often tighter than a process can economically hold. That is the central drawback: worst-case buys absolute assembly assurance at the cost of expensive tolerances, and it treats the simultaneous-extreme event as though it were likely when in fact it is astronomically rare for many independent contributors. Worst-case is therefore the correct and often *mandatory* choice for safety-critical clearances, single-piece or low-volume production where no statistical averaging over a lot exists, mechanisms that must never bind, and any interface where a single non-conforming assembly is unacceptable. For high-volume assemblies with many contributors, the linear sum is usually over-conservative, which is precisely the gap that statistical stack-up methods fill.
