**Convergent (hybrid) modeling** unifies exact boundary representation with faceted mesh geometry inside a *single* model, so that scanned or tessellated data and classical analytic solids can be edited with the same operations. It exists because real engineering data increasingly arrives as meshes: 3D scans and reverse-engineering captures, results of topology optimization, medical imaging isosurfaces, and simulation output. The traditional pipeline forced a costly, error-prone conversion step, refitting a mesh into trimmed NURBS surfaces before any CAD operation could touch it. Hybrid modeling instead treats the mesh as a **first-class geometric carrier** rather than a second-class import.

The enabling idea is to keep the familiar B-rep topological layer (vertices, edges, faces, shells) but allow a face's underlying geometry to be either an analytic/NURBS surface *or* a bounded region of a **triangle mesh (facet body)**. Where an analytic region abuts a facet region, they meet along shared topological edges, so the boundary graph remains connected and consistent across the two kinds of geometry. Supporting the general case, where more than two faces can meet at an edge (mixed patchwork, sheet bodies, internal partitions), requires a **non-manifold** topological data structure such as the radial-edge, which orders the faces radially around each edge and thereby represents manifold solids, open sheets, and non-manifold junctions in one framework.

With that unified topology in place, standard operations must be generalized to act on both geometry kinds and across their interface. Booleans, offsets, shelling, blends/fillets, splits, and mass-property queries all need code paths that handle an analytic face, a facet face, or a boolean whose result cut runs partly through mesh and partly through NURBS. This leans heavily on **tolerant modeling**, because the intersection of an exact surface with a discrete mesh is inherently approximate; edges near the interface carry a tolerance and the operations are designed to stay watertight within it.

The payoff is a workflow where a scanned bracket can be trimmed, filleted, and combined with newly authored analytic pockets without ever refitting the scan to surfaces, and where an optimized mesh from a solver can be finished as a manufacturable part. The trade-offs mirror the underlying geometries: mesh regions do not carry exact analytic parameterizations, so curvature-continuous downstream operations and certain precise references are limited on those faces, and the non-manifold bookkeeping is more complex than a pure two-manifold kernel. Understanding the representation clarifies which operations are exact, which are tolerant approximations, and why the boundary between facet and analytic regions is the delicate part of the model to reason about.
