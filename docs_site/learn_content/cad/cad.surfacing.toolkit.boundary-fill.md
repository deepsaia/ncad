A boundary blend (also called a boundary-fill or Coons-type surface) constructs a single parametric patch that interpolates a closed loop of boundary curves and, optionally, matches the tangency or curvature of the surfaces those curves belong to. It is the workhorse of surface modeling for stitching transitions between disjoint sheets, capping open regions, and lofting across a curve network. The defining requirement is that the resulting surface pass exactly through the given boundaries while behaving predictably in the interior, where no data is prescribed.

The foundational construction is the bilinearly blended Coons patch. Given the two opposite boundary pairs \(\mathbf{c}_0(v),\mathbf{c}_1(v)\) (the edges at \(u=0,1\)) and \(\mathbf{d}_0(u),\mathbf{d}_1(u)\) (the edges at \(v=0,1\)), the patch is the sum of two ruled surfaces minus a bilinear correction term that removes the double-counted corners:

\[
S(u,v) = \begin{bmatrix} 1-u & u \end{bmatrix}\!\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{c}_0(v)\\ \mathbf{c}_1(v) \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{d}_0(u) & \mathbf{d}_1(u) \end{bmatrix}\!\begin{bmatrix} 1-v\\ v \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1-u & u \end{bmatrix}\!\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{P}_{00} & \mathbf{P}_{01}\\ \mathbf{P}_{10} & \mathbf{P}_{11} \end{bmatrix}\!\begin{bmatrix} 1-v\\ v \end{bmatrix}.
\]

The linear blend \((1-u,u)\) can be replaced by cubic Hermite blending functions to obtain a bicubically blended Coons patch, which additionally accepts prescribed cross-boundary tangent (and, with quintic blends, curvature) fields. This is exactly how a boundary blend achieves tangent or curvature continuity to neighbouring faces: the cross-boundary derivatives sampled from the adjacent surfaces become the Hermite tangent data along each edge. When curves are supplied in both parametric directions rather than only on the perimeter, the same idea generalizes to a Gordon surface, which interpolates an entire network of intersecting curves.

## N-sided fill

The Coons/Gordon machinery assumes a four-sided topology, so filling a hole bounded by \(n \neq 4\) edges needs a different strategy. Two families dominate. The first subdivides the \(n\)-gon into four-sided subpatches that meet at an interior point or spine, then enforces internal continuity between the subpatches (a midpoint-split scheme). The second uses a genuine \(n\)-sided formulation built on generalized barycentric or transfinite blending so that every boundary and its cross-boundary field is interpolated simultaneously. In both cases the practical difficulty is not interpolation but interior quality: an unconstrained fill can bulge, ripple, or pinch, so implementations add fairness or minimal-energy objectives to keep the interior smooth.

Boundary fills matter wherever geometry must be closed cleanly without CAD-visible seams: blending the corner where three fillets converge, patching the mouth of a swept pocket, or reconstructing a missing region during reverse engineering. Because the surface is only pinned on its boundary, the continuity conditions requested at those edges (position, tangent, or curvature) directly determine downstream behavior such as reflection quality and machinability, which is why the fill and the continuity specification are always chosen together.
