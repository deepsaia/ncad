A **polygon mesh** approximates a surface with flat facets: a set of **vertices** (points in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)) joined by **edges** into **faces**, most commonly triangles or quadrilaterals. Where a B-rep stores exact curved surfaces, a mesh stores a **piecewise-linear** boundary, trading exactness for uniformity and speed. Meshes are the lingua franca of visualization, physical simulation, digitization, and fabrication precisely because every downstream algorithm (rasterization, ray intersection, finite-element assembly, slicing) reduces to arithmetic over simple polygons.

The minimal storage is an **indexed face set**: an array of vertex coordinates plus an array of faces, each face being a tuple of vertex indices. This shares vertices between faces (avoiding duplication) but stores no adjacency, so neighbor queries are expensive. Processing-oriented representations therefore add connectivity: the **half-edge** structure gives constant-time access to the faces and edges around any vertex, at the cost of memory. A triangle mesh that is a **simplicial complex** (faces meet only along shared full edges and vertices, with no T-junctions or self-intersections) is the well-behaved case most algorithms assume.

Meshes obey the same **Euler formula** as any polyhedral surface, \( V - E + F = 2 - 2g \) for a closed surface of genus \(g\). For a *closed triangle* mesh, every face has three edges and every edge is shared by two faces, so \( 3F = 2E \). Substituting gives the useful rules of thumb

\[ E \approx 3V, \qquad F \approx 2V, \qquad \bar{d} = \frac{2E}{V} \approx 6, \]

meaning a typical vertex has about six incident triangles. These relations let you predict memory and complexity from a single number and are a quick sanity check that a mesh is closed and well-formed.

Several quality properties determine whether a mesh is fit for purpose. **Manifoldness** (each edge shared by at most two faces, each vertex a single fan) and **watertightness** (no boundary edges, no gaps) are prerequisites for solid operations, volume computation, and printable geometry. **Consistent orientation** (all face normals pointing outward, adjacent faces agreeing) is needed for shading and inside/outside tests. Element **shape quality**, measured by aspect ratio or minimum angle, controls the accuracy and conditioning of simulations, since slivers degrade numerical results.

Because facets are linear, a curved surface is only approximated, and the fidelity is governed by **chordal (sag) deviation**, the maximum distance between the facet and the true surface. For a sphere of radius \(R\) tessellated so that a facet spans a chord half-angle, the sag scales with \( R\,\theta^{2}/8 \), so halving the allowed deviation roughly doubles edge count in each direction and quadruples the triangle budget. This is the fundamental tension of meshing: finer meshes look and simulate better but cost memory and compute, which is why adaptive tessellation (dense where curvature is high, coarse where flat) and mesh simplification are standard tools when moving between exact B-reps and their faceted approximations.
