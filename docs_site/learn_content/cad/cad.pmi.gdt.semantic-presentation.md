Product and Manufacturing Information (PMI) can be attached to a 3D model in two fundamentally different ways, and modern data-exchange standards distinguish them explicitly because they serve different consumers. **Presentation PMI** (also called *graphical* or *polyline* PMI) is the human-readable annotation: the feature control frames, dimension text, datum symbols, and leader lines rendered as graphic elements — line segments, arcs, filled text outlines — positioned in model space and grouped into saved views. It reproduces exactly what an engineer would draw, so a reviewer sees the same picture the author intended, but the annotation is essentially a *drawing floating in 3D*: the characters `∅ 0.1 A B C` are geometry, not data a computer can reason about.

**Semantic PMI** (also called *representation* PMI) is the machine-interpretable form: the tolerance is stored as structured data — a typed object that records the characteristic (say, position), the zone value and shape, the material modifier, and explicit associative links to the datum entities and to the exact model faces or edges it governs. Nothing needs to be rendered for the meaning to be recovered; a downstream program can query "what is the position tolerance on hole 7, and what datums does it reference" and get an unambiguous answer. This is what enables automation: coordinate-measuring-machine (CMM) program generation, model-based inspection, automated tolerance stack-up, and manufacturing rule checks all consume semantic PMI, because they need the *logic* of the callout, not its typography.

## Why both, and how they relate

The two representations are complementary rather than redundant. Presentation guarantees fidelity of communication (the annotation looks right and sits where the author placed it); semantics guarantees fidelity of meaning (the annotation *means* one thing to every downstream tool). A robust model-based-definition dataset carries **both**, ideally cross-linked so a graphic annotation and its semantic object are two views of the same fact. The neutral CAD exchange standard STEP AP242 was created in large part to carry semantic PMI with geometric associativity, alongside a presentation form, so that PMI survives translation between systems instead of degrading to unlinked text.

The practical failure mode to avoid is *presentation-only* data: annotations that display correctly but have no semantic backing and no associativity to geometry. Such a model looks complete to a human but is opaque to automation, forcing manual re-keying that reintroduces exactly the transcription errors model-based definition set out to eliminate. Best practice — and the reason the distinction is called out in standards — is to author semantic PMI first (so the meaning and geometric links exist), then derive or attach the presentation for human review. When only one can be provided, semantic is the more valuable of the two for any downstream digital thread; presentation is what makes the model usable at a glance on the shop floor.
