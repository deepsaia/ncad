A NURBS surface is the two-parameter generalization of the NURBS curve, built by the *tensor-product* construction. Two independent parameter directions \(u\) and \(v\) each carry their own degree (\(p\) and \(q\)) and knot vector, and the control points form a rectangular grid \(\mathbf{P}_{i,j}\) with weights \(w_{i,j}\). The surface is
\[ \mathbf{S}(u,v) = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{n}\sum_{j=0}^{m} N_{i,p}(u)\,N_{j,q}(v)\,w_{i,j}\,\mathbf{P}_{i,j}}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{n}\sum_{j=0}^{m} N_{i,p}(u)\,N_{j,q}(v)\,w_{i,j}}. \]
The basis is literally the product of a \(u\)-basis and a \(v\)-basis, which is what "tensor product" means: fixing one parameter and sweeping the other traces a NURBS *curve* on the surface, called an *isoparametric* (iso) curve. Consequently every property of NURBS curves - convex hull, local control, projective invariance, exact conics - lifts to the surface, and evaluation reduces to applying the curve algorithms once in each direction.

## Trimming and the connection to B-rep faces

The tensor-product form intrinsically covers a *rectangular* \((u,v)\) domain, but real part faces are rarely rectangular: they have holes, curved boundaries, and irregular outlines. The standard solution is a *trimmed* surface. The underlying NURBS carries the geometry, while a set of *trim curves* defined in the surface's 2D parameter space delineates which region of the domain is actually kept. A face in a boundary-representation (B-rep) solid is therefore a pairing of one untrimmed NURBS surface with an ordered loop of parameter-space trim curves, and this pairing is the fundamental building block of solid models.

Several practical behaviors follow from the grid structure. Continuity across a knot line is governed by knot multiplicity in that direction just as for curves, so a surface can be smooth in \(u\) yet only tangent-continuous in \(v\). *Degenerate* edges arise when an entire row or column of control points collapses to a single point, producing a *pole* (a singular point where the normal is undefined) - the apex of a cone or the caps of a sphere are the familiar cases, and downstream algorithms must guard against the vanishing partial derivative there. Because the weights admit exact quadrics, primitive surfaces (planes, cylinders, cones, spheres, tori) all have exact NURBS forms, which is again why the representation serves as the universal interchange for surface geometry.
