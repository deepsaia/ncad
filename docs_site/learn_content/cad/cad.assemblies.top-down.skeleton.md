In **top-down design**, the shared, load-bearing decisions of a product are captured centrally *before* individual parts are detailed, and the parts are then built to conform to that central definition. The artifact that holds those decisions is the **skeleton** (sometimes an assembly-level layout), and the broader principle it embodies is the **master model**: a single authoritative source of the geometry and dimensions that multiple downstream models must agree on. This inverts the bottom-up habit of designing parts first and reconciling their interfaces afterward, which scales poorly and tends to leave interface definitions duplicated and inconsistent across parts.

A skeleton is typically a lightweight, largely mass-free model containing only reference geometry and key dimensions: datum planes, axes, and points that establish coordinate frames; the critical lengths, angles, and offsets that define the product's layout; the location and orientation of interfaces between components; and motion envelopes or clearance volumes. Detailed parts do not invent their own copies of these; they *reference* the skeleton, so a bolt-circle diameter or a pivot location lives in exactly one place. When the skeleton changes, every part built against it is driven to update accordingly.

## The master-model idea

The master-model concept generalizes this: there is one master geometric model, and application-specific views, such as an analysis idealization, a manufacturing model, or an assembly layout, are *derived* from it and kept consistent through it rather than maintained independently. The value is that design intent that is genuinely global to the product, its interfaces and controlling dimensions, is expressed once and consumed many times, which is precisely what keeps a large model internally consistent as it evolves.

## Where it matters

Skeletons and master models are essential on large, multi-team products and in concurrent engineering, where different groups develop mating components in parallel. By publishing the interface layout centrally, a team can proceed against a stable contract, interfaces stay aligned, and a change to a controlling dimension propagates predictably rather than requiring a manual sweep through dozens of parts. The discipline also helps avoid the circular and fragile inter-part references that arise when parts reference each other's incidental geometry directly.
