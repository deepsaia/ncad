A system of differential equations is called **stiff** when it contains phenomena evolving on widely separated time scales, so that certain solution components decay much faster than the component you actually want to track. The practical symptom, first named by Curtiss and Hirschfelder, is that an explicit integrator is forced to take absurdly small steps for *stability* long after those fast components have died out and stopped contributing anything to *accuracy*. Stiffness is not a property of the exact solution alone; it is a property of the equation together with the interval and the accuracy demanded. It is best understood as a mismatch between the scales present and the scales you care about.

## Diagnosing stiffness through the Jacobian

Linearizing \(\dot y = F(t,y)\) about the current state gives the Jacobian \(J=\partial F/\partial y\), whose eigenvalues \(\lambda_i\) set the local time scales: each \(\lambda_i\) with negative real part corresponds to a mode decaying like \(e^{\Re(\lambda_i)\,t}\). A common quantitative indicator is the **stiffness ratio**

\[ S = \frac{\max_i |\Re(\lambda_i)|}{\min_i |\Re(\lambda_i)|}, \]

taken over eigenvalues with \(\Re(\lambda_i)<0\). When \(S\) reaches \(10^3\)-\(10^6\) or more, the problem is strongly stiff. An explicit method must keep \(h\,\lambda_i\) inside its bounded stability region for *every* eigenvalue, including the fastest, so \(h \lesssim c/\max_i|\Re(\lambda_i)|\); meanwhile the solution of interest changes on the slow scale \(1/\min_i|\Re(\lambda_i)|\). The number of steps to cross the interval then scales with \(S\), which is ruinous.

## Where stiffness comes from in multibody systems

Mechanical models generate stiffness readily. Stiff springs and structural members give high natural frequencies \(\omega=\sqrt{k/m}\), whose eigenvalues sit far out on the imaginary axis (or in the left half-plane once damping is added). Compliant joints, bushings, and rubber mounts add fast damped modes. Penalty or regularized contact introduces very large contact stiffnesses precisely to approximate a rigid constraint. Enforcing holonomic constraints by stiff penalty terms rather than exact Lagrange multipliers, or stabilizing constraint drift by Baumgarte feedback with large gains, also injects fast modes. In each case the fast dynamics are physically incidental (you want the slow rigid-body or low-frequency motion) yet they dictate the explicit step.

## What stiff solvers require

Because the fast modes decay, the ideal integrator damps them without needing to resolve them, which demands stability across the whole left half-plane. The relevant notions are **A-stability** (stability region contains all of \(\Re(z)\le 0\)) and the stronger **L-stability**, where additionally the amplification factor \(R(z)\to 0\) as \(\Re(z)\to-\infty\), so extremely stiff modes are annihilated in one step rather than merely bounded. Backward Euler is L-stable; the trapezoidal rule is A-stable but not L-stable (it leaves stiff modes ringing near amplitude one). Practical stiff solvers use backward differentiation formulas (BDF, e.g. the classic Gear methods), L-stable implicit Runge-Kutta schemes such as Radau IIA, or Rosenbrock methods. A hard constraint limits how far one can push linear multistep methods: the **Dahlquist second barrier** states that no A-stable linear multistep method can exceed order 2, which is why higher-order stiff integration turns to implicit Runge-Kutta or to BDF orders that trade a little stability for accuracy.

The cost is that every implicit step needs a Newton solve and hence the Jacobian \(J\) and a factorization of \(I - h\gamma J\). Efficient stiff solvers therefore reuse and only occasionally refactor the Jacobian, exploit its sparsity (in multibody DAEs it has clear block structure), and adapt both step size and order from local error estimates. The overall lesson is architectural: identify whether a model is stiff before choosing an integrator, because on a stiff problem an explicit method is not merely slower, it can be slower by many orders of magnitude.
