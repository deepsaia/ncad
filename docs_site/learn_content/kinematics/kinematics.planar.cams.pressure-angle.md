Once a cam's motion program is fixed, two geometric choices determine whether the mechanism actually runs well: the **follower type** and the resulting **pressure angle**. The follower is the member that rides on the cam and delivers the output motion, and its geometry sets both the contact conditions and how the prescribed displacement maps onto the physical cam profile.

## Follower types

Followers are classified by their contact surface and by their motion. By surface: a **knife-edge** follower is a theoretical idealization used in layout but impractical because of contact stress and wear; a **roller** follower replaces sliding contact with rolling and is the most common practical choice; a **flat-face** (mushroom) follower tolerates high pressure angles and is favored in engine valve gear; and a **spherical-face** follower is used where slight misalignment must be accommodated. By motion, the follower may **translate** along a straight line (radial, if that line passes through the cam axis, or **offset** if it does not) or it may **oscillate** about a fixed pivot as a pivoted arm. Finally, contact must be maintained: a **force-closed** cam relies on a spring (or gravity) to hold the follower against the cam, while a **form-closed** (desmodromic or grooved) cam captures the follower in a track so that the cam positively drives it in both directions.

## Pressure angle

The **pressure angle** \(\phi\) is the angle between the direction of the follower's motion and the common normal to the two contacting surfaces at the point of contact, that is, the line along which the cam pushes on the follower. Only the component of the contact force along the direction of motion does useful work; the perpendicular component is a side thrust that must be reacted by the follower guide or pivot bearing. A large pressure angle therefore means large side loads, high guide friction, deflection, and, in the extreme, self-locking or jamming of a translating follower. A common design limit is to keep \(\phi\) below roughly \(30^\circ\) for translating followers, with somewhat larger values tolerable for oscillating or flat-face followers.

For a radial or offset translating roller follower, the pressure angle at cam angle \(\theta\) is

\[ \tan\phi = \frac{\dfrac{ds}{d\theta} - \varepsilon}{\,s + \sqrt{R_b^2 - \varepsilon^2}\,}, \]

where \(s(\theta)\) is the follower displacement, \(ds/d\theta\) its geometric velocity, \(R_b\) the **base-circle** radius (the smallest circle of the cam), and \(\varepsilon\) the follower offset. The key practical consequence is visible in the denominator: **enlarging the base circle reduces the pressure angle** everywhere along the rise, because the same lift is spread over a physically larger cam. Designers thus trade cam size against pressure angle, and use follower offset (of the correct sign) to shave the peak pressure angle on the more heavily loaded stroke. Flat-face followers sidestep the issue differently, since their contact normal is always parallel to the follower motion, giving a nominally constant pressure angle but instead requiring a check that the profile stays convex to avoid undercutting.
