The Scotch yoke (also called a slotted-link or crank-and-slot mechanism) converts continuous rotation into reciprocating translation, and it is distinguished by producing motion that is *exactly* simple harmonic rather than merely approximately so. A pin on a rotating crank of radius \(r\) engages a straight slot cut transverse to the sliding axis in a yoke; the yoke is constrained to move along one line only. As the crank turns, the pin slides up and down the slot while pushing the yoke back and forth.

## Pure simple harmonic motion

Because the slot is perpendicular to the direction of yoke travel, the yoke displacement equals the crank pin's projection onto the sliding axis. With the crank turning at constant angular speed \(\omega\) and \(\theta = \omega t\),

\[ x = r\cos\omega t, \qquad \dot{x} = -r\omega\sin\omega t, \qquad \ddot{x} = -r\omega^2\cos\omega t = -\omega^2 x. \]

The acceleration is proportional to displacement and directed toward the center, the defining property of simple harmonic motion. This is the fundamental contrast with the slider-crank: the finite connecting rod of a slider-crank injects a second-harmonic \(\cos 2\theta\) term into its acceleration, whereas the Scotch yoke has no connecting rod and therefore no higher harmonics at all. Effectively it is the limiting case of a slider-crank with an infinitely long rod, but achieved with a compact slotted member.

## Uses and trade-offs

The pure sinusoid makes the Scotch yoke valuable wherever clean harmonic motion is wanted: mechanical **sine/cosine generators** and function tables, shaker tables and vibration test rigs, certain reciprocating pumps and compressors, and quarter-turn **valve actuators**, where the same geometry run in reverse turns linear piston force into a torque with a favorable, near-constant profile over the mid-stroke. Its single moving contact and short overall length give a smaller, lighter reciprocating assembly than an equivalent slider-crank, and the absence of side thrust from a tilting rod can reduce guide loading.

The practical price is the sliding pin-in-slot contact. The pin bears against the slot walls under the full inertial and working load along a small contact patch that reverses direction twice per revolution, so wear, lubrication, and contact stress at that interface are the limiting design concerns, often mitigated with a rolling follower or a hardened, well-lubricated slot. Kinematically the mechanism is straightforward (one degree of freedom, two dead-center positions at the stroke ends), and its value lies precisely in the mathematically exact harmonic output that its slotted geometry guarantees.
