Gears transmit rotation between shafts by meshing teeth, and the entire quality of that transmission rests on the shapes of the tooth flanks. The requirement that makes a gear a *gear* rather than a lumpy friction wheel is a smooth, constant speed ratio, and the geometric statement of that requirement is the **fundamental law of gearing** (the law of conjugate action).

## The fundamental law of gearing

Consider two tooth profiles in contact. Draw the **common normal** to both surfaces at the contact point; this is the line along which the tooth force acts. The fundamental law states that for the angular velocity ratio of the two gears to remain constant, the common normal at every point of contact must pass through a single fixed point on the line connecting the two gear centers. That point is the **pitch point** \(P\). If \(P\) divides the center distance into radii \(r_2\) and \(r_3\), then

\[ \frac{\omega_2}{\omega_3} = \frac{r_3}{r_2} = \text{constant}. \]

Equivalently, the pitch point splits the center distance in the inverse ratio of the angular speeds. Any pair of tooth profiles that satisfies this condition are called *conjugate*; the pitch point is where the two imaginary friction cylinders (pitch circles) that would produce the same ratio roll without slipping.

## Why the involute

Many conjugate profiles exist, but the **involute of a circle** is used almost universally because it satisfies the law with uniquely convenient properties. An involute is the curve traced by the end of a taut string as it unwinds from a **base circle** of radius \(r_b\). When two involutes mesh, the locus of contact points, the **line of action**, is the straight common tangent to the two base circles, and the tooth force acts along this fixed line at a constant **pressure angle** \(\alpha\) (standardized at \(20^\circ\), historically \(14.5^\circ\)). The base and pitch radii are related by

\[ r_b = r\cos\alpha, \]

and the geometry is described by the involute function \(\operatorname{inv}\alpha = \tan\alpha - \alpha\), which underlies tooth-thickness and span-measurement calculations.

The decisive practical virtue of the involute is its insensitivity to center distance. Because the profile depends only on the base circle, moving the shafts slightly farther apart merely enlarges the operating pitch circles and pressure angle in exactly the ratio needed to keep the velocity ratio constant, so involute gears tolerate assembly and bearing tolerances without transmission error. Involutes are also generated by a straight-sided (rack) cutter, which makes precise, low-cost manufacture by hobbing and shaping possible and gives interchangeability among gears of the same module and pressure angle. Smooth running additionally requires that contact never be lost, quantified by the **contact ratio** (the average number of tooth pairs in mesh, the length of the line of action divided by the base pitch), which must exceed one and is usually kept above about 1.2 to 1.4.
