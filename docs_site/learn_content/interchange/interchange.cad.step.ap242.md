STEP (STandard for the Exchange of Product model data, ISO 10303) is the international framework for exchanging product data between systems without depending on any one vendor's internal representation. Within STEP, an *Application Protocol* (AP) is a formally specified subset that scopes which entities are used for a particular engineering domain. **AP242, "Managed model-based 3D engineering,"** consolidates and supersedes the two protocols that dominated mechanical exchange for two decades: AP203 (configuration-controlled 3D design, rooted in aerospace) and AP214 (automotive core data). Its purpose is to be a single, upward-compatible protocol carrying not just shape, but the full engineering definition, geometry, product structure, configuration, and manufacturing information, so that the 3D model itself becomes the authoritative deliverable (the *model-based definition*, MBD) rather than a 2D drawing.

Every STEP protocol is built on a data model written in the EXPRESS schema language (ISO 10303-11) and is serialized in the Part 21 clear-text encoding, the familiar `.stp`/`.step` file, or in an XML form. Exact geometry is carried as boundary representation (B-rep): topological entities (`closed_shell`, `advanced_face`, `edge_loop`, `vertex_point`) bound to geometric carriers such as `b_spline_surface_with_knots` and trimmed NURBS. Because the schema is neutral and formally typed, a conforming reader reconstructs the same manifold solid a conforming writer exported, which is the guarantee IGES never offered.

## PMI: presentation versus representation

AP242's headline capability is **Product Manufacturing Information (PMI)**: geometric dimensioning and tolerancing (GD&T), datums, surface finish, weld symbols, and notes. PMI exists in two forms, and the distinction matters. *Presentation* (graphical) PMI stores annotations as styled polylines pinned to saved views, it looks right to a human but is not machine-interpretable. *Representation* (semantic) PMI encodes each tolerance as structured data tied to the actual geometric faces it controls, so a downstream process, a CMM inspection plan or CAM feature recognizer, can consume the tolerance without a person re-keying it. Best practice publishes both, and AP242's rigorous semantic PMI is precisely why it underpins MBD and long-term archival (LOTAR) programs.

## Assembly structure and the extended-data-exchange layer

Assemblies are represented as a product-structure graph: `next_assembly_usage_occurrence` relations link component definitions, and each occurrence carries a placement. A placement is given by an `axis2_placement_3d`, an origin \(p\), a local z-axis \(a\), and a reference direction \(r\), from which an orthonormal basis is recovered by Gram-Schmidt:

\[ \hat{z} = \frac{a}{\lVert a \rVert}, \qquad \hat{x} = \frac{r-(r\cdot\hat{z})\,\hat{z}}{\lVert r-(r\cdot\hat{z})\,\hat{z}\rVert}, \qquad \hat{y} = \hat{z}\times\hat{x}. \]

The component's rigid placement is the homogeneous transform \(T=\begin{bmatrix}\hat{x}&\hat{y}&\hat{z}&p\\0&0&0&1\end{bmatrix}\), and a nested instance's world pose is the ordered product \(T_{\text{world}}=T_1 T_2\cdots T_n\) down the tree.

Carrying all of this reliably across a translator requires an *extended data exchange* layer, exemplified by the XDE/XCAF framework, that maps STEP entities onto a document with a label tree. Rather than reducing an import to a bag of loose faces, this layer preserves the assembly hierarchy, per-instance placements, colors, layers, names, and the PMI/GD&T attachments as first-class, addressable nodes. It is the practical mechanism by which non-geometric intent, the part of AP242 that distinguishes it from mere shape transfer, actually survives a round trip.
