Thermal-structural (thermomechanical) coupling links a temperature field to the stresses and deformations it produces. The physical driver is **thermal expansion**: a temperature change \(\Delta T = T - T_{\text{ref}}\) above a stress-free reference makes an unconstrained material grow. For an isotropic solid the induced **thermal strain** is purely dilatational,

\[ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^{\text{th}} = \alpha\,\Delta T\,\mathbf{I}, \]

with \(\alpha\) the coefficient of thermal expansion. Free expansion produces no stress; **stress arises only when expansion is restrained**, either by external constraints or by neighboring material at a different temperature. This is captured by an additive strain decomposition in which the elastic (stress-producing) strain is the total mechanical strain minus the thermal part,

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma} = \mathbf{D}\,\big(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} - \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^{\text{th}}\big) = \mathbf{D}\,(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} - \alpha\,\Delta T\,\mathbf{I}), \]

where \(\mathbf{D}\) is the elasticity matrix. The canonical illustration is a bar rigidly clamped at both ends and heated uniformly: it cannot elongate, so its total strain is zero and the axial stress is the fully compressive \(\sigma = -E\,\alpha\,\Delta T\), independent of length. This is why constrained structures buckle rails, crack weld heat-affected zones, and warp electronic assemblies made of materials with mismatched \(\alpha\).

## Sequential (one-way) coupling

In most engineering practice the coupling is **one-way**: the temperature field affects the mechanical response, but the small amount of heat generated by deformation is negligible. The workflow is sequential. First solve the thermal problem, \(\mathbf{K}_t\,\mathbf{T} = \mathbf{F}_t\), for the nodal temperatures. Then map that field onto the structural mesh and assemble an equivalent **thermal load vector** from the eigenstrain,

\[ \mathbf{F}^{\text{th}} = \int_V \mathbf{B}^{\mathsf T}\,\mathbf{D}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^{\text{th}}\,dV, \]

and solve the elasticity problem \(\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{F}_{\text{mech}} + \mathbf{F}^{\text{th}}\). The thermal expansion acts exactly like a distributed body load. Using a common mesh (or a consistent temperature interpolation between meshes) matters here, because interpolation error in \(\Delta T\) becomes stress error directly through the gradient of the temperature field.

## Two-way (fully coupled) coupling

When the mechanical and thermal fields feed back on one another, the analysis must be **fully coupled**, solving temperature and displacement simultaneously in a monolithic system

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{K}_{uu} & \mathbf{K}_{uT} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{K}_{TT} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \mathbf{u} \\ \mathbf{T} \end{Bmatrix} + \dots = \begin{Bmatrix} \mathbf{F}_u \\ \mathbf{F}_T \end{Bmatrix}, \]

where the off-diagonal blocks carry the mutual influence. Two-way coupling is required when deformation changes the heat problem: thermoelastic damping and the Gough-Joule effect (heating under adiabatic straining), frictional or plastic-work heating in forming and crash, contact whose conductance depends on pressure, and any case where large displacement reshapes convection or contact interfaces. The trade-off is cost and conditioning, since the coupled operator is generally nonsymmetric and often nonlinear (temperature-dependent \(E\), \(\alpha\), and yield). A disciplined analyst therefore starts with the cheapest defensible model, sequential one-way coupling with a stress-free reference temperature, and escalates to full coupling only where the physics or the accuracy target demands it.
