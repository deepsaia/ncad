Turbulence is the defining challenge of practical CFD. A turbulent flow contains a continuous cascade of eddies spanning many orders of magnitude in size, from the large energy-containing structures set by the geometry down to the tiny **Kolmogorov scale** \( \eta = (\nu^3/\varepsilon)^{1/4} \) at which viscosity dissipates kinetic energy into heat. The ratio of largest to smallest scales grows with Reynolds number, and resolving every scale everywhere is prohibitively expensive for most engineering flows. The three canonical approaches (DNS, LES, RANS) differ precisely in how much of that spectrum they resolve directly versus model.

## DNS: resolve everything

**Direct Numerical Simulation** solves the unaveraged Navier-Stokes equations on a grid fine enough and a time step small enough to capture all scales down to \( \eta \). It introduces no turbulence model and is effectively a numerical experiment. The catch is cost: the number of grid points needed scales as \( N \sim Re^{9/4} \) in three dimensions, and total computational effort scales roughly as \( Re^3 \) once time-step resolution is included. This confines DNS to low-to-moderate Reynolds numbers and simple geometries, where it serves as a source of benchmark data and physical insight rather than as a routine design tool.

## LES: resolve the large eddies, model the small

**Large Eddy Simulation** applies a spatial filter of width \( \Delta \) to the equations, directly resolving eddies larger than the filter and modeling only the subgrid-scale (SGS) motions. Because the small scales are more universal and isotropic than the large ones, they are easier to model reliably. Filtering the momentum equation produces an SGS stress \( \tau_{ij}^{sgs} = \overline{u_i u_j} - \bar{u}_i \bar{u}_j \) that must be closed; the classic Smagorinsky model uses an eddy viscosity \( \nu_{sgs} = (C_s \Delta)^2 |\bar{S}| \) proportional to the resolved strain rate. LES captures unsteady, three-dimensional structures that steady models miss (separation, large-scale mixing, aeroacoustic sources) at a cost far below DNS but still well above steady modeling, especially because near walls the energetic eddies shrink and the resolution requirement approaches that of DNS.

## RANS: model everything, resolve the mean

**Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes** decomposes each field into a mean and a fluctuation, \( u_i = \bar{u}_i + u_i' \), and averages the equations. The nonlinear convective term generates the unclosed **Reynolds stress** \( -\rho\, \overline{u_i' u_j'} \), which represents the net momentum transport by turbulent fluctuations. This is the **closure problem**: there are more unknowns than equations. The most common closures invoke the Boussinesq eddy-viscosity hypothesis,

\[ -\rho\, \overline{u_i' u_j'} = \mu_t\!\left(\frac{\partial \bar{u}_i}{\partial x_j} + \frac{\partial \bar{u}_j}{\partial x_i}\right) - \frac{2}{3}\rho k\, \delta_{ij} \]

and supply transport equations for turbulence quantities to compute the eddy viscosity \( \mu_t \). Two-equation models such as \( k\text{-}\varepsilon \) and \( k\text{-}\omega \) (and blended forms that use \( k\text{-}\omega \) near walls and \( k\text{-}\varepsilon \) in the free stream) are the workhorses of industrial CFD. RANS resolves only the mean flow, so its cost is essentially independent of Reynolds number and orders of magnitude below LES.

The practical trade-off is accuracy versus cost. RANS is fast and robust and is the default for design-space exploration, but its single-length-scale eddy-viscosity assumption struggles with strong separation, swirl, and unsteady wakes. LES resolves those phenomena at much higher cost, and hybrid strategies (attaching RANS in the near-wall boundary layer to LES away from it) are widely used to make the resolved-eddy approach affordable at engineering Reynolds numbers. DNS remains the gold-standard reference. Choosing among them is a resource-versus-fidelity decision that should be made deliberately for the physics that matters in a given problem.
