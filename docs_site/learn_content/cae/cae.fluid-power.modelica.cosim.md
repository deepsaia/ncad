Modelica is an open, equation-based, object-oriented modeling language for multi-domain physical systems. Its central abstraction is the **lumped-parameter** (0-D) model: continuous fields are collapsed into discrete elements characterized by a few parameters, so a hydraulic line becomes a resistance-plus-capacitance, a shaft becomes an inertia, a chamber becomes a fluid capacitance. Unlike block-diagram tools that require the modeler to pre-solve for signal flow, Modelica is **acausal**: a component states its constitutive equations (for example a resistor as \(v = R\,i\)) without committing to which variables are inputs or outputs, and the compiler decides causality when the whole system is assembled. This lets one component model serve in many contexts and makes physical topology, not signal wiring, the thing the engineer draws.

## Connectors, conservation, and the flat DAE

Components join at **connectors** carrying paired variables: an **across** (potential) variable such as pressure, voltage, or velocity, and a **through** (flow) variable such as volumetric flow, current, or force. Connection semantics enforce the domain's conservation laws automatically, potentials are set equal at a node and flow variables sum to zero, a generalized Kirchhoff / d'Alembert statement, \[ p_1 = p_2 = \dots = p_n, \qquad \sum_{k=1}^{n} \dot{m}_k = 0. \] Flattening the connected model produces a large system of **differential-algebraic equations (DAEs)** of the form \[ F\big(\dot{x}(t),\,x(t),\,y(t),\,u(t),\,t\big) = 0, \] mixing differential states \(x\) with algebraic unknowns \(y\). A Modelica compiler performs symbolic manipulation, alias elimination, tearing of algebraic loops, and **structural index reduction** (Pantelides' algorithm with dummy derivatives) to turn a high-index DAE into a numerically integrable index-1 or ODE form before a solver (typically an implicit, stiffly-stable BDF or Runge-Kutta method) advances it in time.

## Co-simulation and the FMI contract

Real engineering problems span tools and teams: a hydraulic circuit built in one environment must run against a controller, a structural model, or a mechanism authored elsewhere. **Co-simulation** couples such independently developed and independently integrated subsystems, each keeping its own internal solver and state, exchanging data only at discrete **communication points** \(t_c\) spaced by a macro (communication) step \(H\). This is standardized product-neutrally by the **Functional Mock-up Interface (FMI)**, which packages a model as a Functional Mock-up Unit (FMU) exposing a C API to set inputs, `doStep(t_c, H)`, and get outputs. A **master algorithm** orchestrates the FMUs, choosing \(H\), the ordering of exchanges (Jacobi vs. Gauss-Seidel), and whether steps can be rolled back and repeated. Because each subsystem is a black box between communication points, inputs are held or extrapolated across the macro-step, which injects a coupling error and can destabilize the coupled system if \(H\) is too large relative to the fastest coupled dynamics.

## Why it matters and where it is used

The lumped, equation-based approach trades spatial detail for breadth and speed: a whole hydraulic-mechanical-electrical machine can be simulated far faster than any 3-D field solve, which is exactly what system-level design, control tuning, and hardware-in-the-loop testing need. In a mechanism or motion context, a fluid-power or controls model can act as a **driver source**, computing actuator forces or prescribed displacements that are fed, through the FMI co-simulation contract, into a multibody or motion solver each communication step. The result is a co-simulation in which the physically faithful actuator dynamics (with their compressibility spring and low damping) shape the motion, rather than an idealized rigid input, while each domain is solved by the method best suited to it.
