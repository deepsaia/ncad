Fluid power is the transmission and control of energy through a pressurized fluid: an (almost) incompressible liquid in hydraulics, or a compressible gas in pneumatics. A circuit converts mechanical shaft power into fluid power at a **pump** (or compressor), routes and modulates that power through **valves** and conductors, and converts it back into linear or rotary mechanical work at an **actuator** (a cylinder or a fluid motor). The appeal of the approach is force density: because pressure acts on an area, a modestly sized cylinder can produce very large forces, and the working fluid simultaneously lubricates, cools, and can be routed flexibly through hoses. The behavior of any single-fluid circuit rests on three ideas: pressure is set by the load, flow is set by the source, and every restriction trades pressure for control.

## Sources, actuators, and Pascal's law

Most fluid-power systems use **positive-displacement** pumps, which deliver a fixed volume of fluid per revolution. Ideal delivered flow is therefore \[ Q = D\,n \] where \(D\) is the volumetric displacement and \(n\) the shaft speed; real pumps lose a slip flow roughly proportional to pressure. A positive-displacement pump is fundamentally a **flow source**: it will raise pressure to whatever the downstream load demands, which is why a relief valve is mandatory to cap system pressure. Pressure acts through **Pascal's law**: a pressure change applied to a confined fluid is transmitted undiminished, so a piston of area \(A\) develops force \(F = pA\) and moves at velocity \(v = Q/A\). The hydraulic power crossing any section is the product of pressure and flow, \(P = p\,Q\), which is the invariant that circuit design must budget against.

## Valves: directing, limiting, and metering

Valves are the control elements and fall into three families. **Directional-control valves** (spool or poppet) route flow to select actuator direction, hold, or float. **Pressure-control valves** (relief, reducing, sequence, counterbalance) reference a spring or pilot line to set a pressure threshold. **Flow-control valves** meter velocity by throttling area. The workhorse relation for a throttling edge is the turbulent **orifice equation**, \[ Q = C_d\,A_o\sqrt{\tfrac{2\,\Delta p}{\rho}} \] where \(C_d\) is the discharge coefficient (\(\approx 0.6\text{–}0.7\)), \(A_o\) the metering area, \(\Delta p\) the pressure drop, and \(\rho\) the fluid density. This nonlinearity, flow scaling with the square root of pressure drop, dominates circuit tuning: metering out on the exhaust side of a cylinder stabilizes an overrunning load, whereas metering in leaves it prone to runaway.

## Hydraulic versus pneumatic

The incompressibility of hydraulic fluid gives stiff, precisely positionable, high-force actuation at pressures of tens of megapascals, at the cost of contamination sensitivity and leakage. Pneumatics trade that stiffness for the compressibility of gas: air stores energy as it is compressed, so pneumatic actuators are compliant and hard to position accurately mid-stroke but are clean, fast, and inherently overload-tolerant. Compressibility also changes the governing physics, high-pressure-ratio flow through a valve becomes **choked** (sonic) when downstream-to-upstream pressure falls below the critical ratio (about 0.528 for air), so mass flow saturates and no longer depends on downstream pressure. Standardized graphical symbols and circuit-diagram conventions (per ISO 1219) let engineers document either medium in a product-neutral, tool-independent schematic, which is the shared language for analyzing and troubleshooting these systems.
