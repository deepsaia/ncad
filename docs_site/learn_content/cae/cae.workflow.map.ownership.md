In a modern computer-aided-engineering (CAE) pipeline the *authoring* of a simulation and the *numerical solution* of it are two distinct responsibilities, and it is a deliberate architectural choice to keep them separate. The modeling application is the single source of truth for the **analysis model**: the geometry (or the idealized version of it), the material assignments, the mesh and mesh controls, the loads and boundary conditions, the contact definitions, and the solution steps. What it deliberately does *not* own is the solver itself. Instead it emits a self-contained description of the problem and hands it to a specialized engine that runs the numerics. This is the same separation classical finite-element texts describe as **pre-processing, solution, and post-processing**: the pre-processor defines the discrete problem, the solver assembles and solves it, and the post-processor interprets the answer. Keeping these as separate stages, rather than one monolith, is what makes the workflow auditable and reproducible.

The reason the split exists is partly technical and partly organizational. A production solver for structural, thermal, fluid, or electromagnetic physics is an enormous, heavily validated body of code with its own numerical subtleties (element formulations, integration schemes, contact algorithms, iterative solvers, convergence controls). Different physics and different regimes demand different engines, and those engines are often licensed per-seat or per-core, scheduled onto high-performance-computing (HPC) clusters, and run as batch jobs far from the interactive workstation. By treating the solver as a swappable, external dependency, the modeling layer stays **solver-agnostic**: the same declarative model can, in principle, target more than one engine, and the engine can be upgraded or moved to a cluster without changing how the part is authored.

The governing idea is that the *model is declarative and persistent* while the *solve is a transient pure function*. The model is a parametric, editable, version-controllable artifact that captures engineering intent. The solve, by contrast, is a computation whose only inputs are the exported problem definition and the solver's own settings; given the same inputs it should produce the same outputs. For a linear static structural problem, for instance, the entire hand-off reduces to the engine assembling and solving

\[ \mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f}, \]

where the stiffness matrix \( \mathbf{K} \), the load vector \( \mathbf{f} \), and the constrained degrees of freedom are *entirely determined by the model the authoring tool owns*. The solver contributes the factorization and the solution \( \mathbf{u} \); it does not contribute meaning. This clean boundary is what lets the same model be re-solved years later, on a different machine, and be expected to agree.

## Why the boundary matters

Practically, the ownership split has consequences for provenance and quality control. Because the model is the source of truth, it is the model (not the multi-gigabyte result file) that belongs under configuration management; results are regenerated from it on demand. Because the solve is delegated, failures have a clear locus: a malformed problem is the authoring layer's fault, a non-convergence or numerical breakdown is diagnosed from the solver's log, and a wrong-but-valid answer points at the modeling assumptions (mesh density, boundary conditions, material data). This mirrors how professional CAE teams already work: analysts speak of "building the model" and "running the job" as separate acts, and the deliverable that is reviewed and reused is the model plus a record of which solver and settings produced a given result set.
