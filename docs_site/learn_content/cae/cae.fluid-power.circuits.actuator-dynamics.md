Actuator dynamics is the study of how a fluid-power actuator responds in time, not merely what steady force or speed it can hold. The static picture (\(F=pA\), \(v=Q/A\)) hides the two energy stores that make a real actuator oscillate and lag: the **inertia** of the moving mass and the **compliance** of the trapped fluid. A valve-controlled cylinder is therefore a coupled electro-fluid-mechanical system whose analysis pairs a flow-continuity equation for the fluid volumes with Newton's second law for the piston. Getting this coupling right is what separates a servo axis that tracks a command crisply from one that rings, stalls, or goes unstable under a control loop.

## Compressibility gives the fluid a spring rate

Even "incompressible" oil deforms slightly under pressure, quantified by the **effective bulk modulus** \(\beta_e = -V\,\dfrac{dp}{dV}\), typically 1–1.7 GPa but sharply reduced by entrained air or compliant hoses. Applying mass continuity to a cylinder chamber, the flow supplied must account for piston motion, fluid compression, and leakage: \[ Q_L = A\,\dot{x} + \frac{V_t}{4\beta_e}\,\dot{p}_L + C_{tp}\,p_L \] where \(A\) is piston area, \(x\) position, \(p_L\) the load pressure, \(V_t\) the total trapped volume, and \(C_{tp}\) a leakage coefficient. The compressibility term is the hydraulic spring: a column of fluid of stiffness \(k_h = \dfrac{4\beta_e A^2}{V_t}\) sits between the valve and the load. Because \(V_t\) grows as the piston nears mid-stroke, this stiffness (and the resonance built on it) is position-dependent, which is a defining, and often troublesome, feature of hydraulic servos.

## The core third-order model

Closing the loop with Newton's law on the piston mass \(m\), \[ A\,p_L = m\,\ddot{x} + b\,\dot{x} + K_s\,x + F_L, \] and linearizing the valve flow about an operating point as \(Q_L = K_q\,x_v - K_c\,p_L\) (flow gain \(K_q\), flow-pressure coefficient \(K_c\)), yields the classic third-order transfer function from spool position \(x_v\) to piston position \(x\). Its dynamics are dominated by the **hydraulic natural frequency** and **damping ratio** \[ \omega_h = \sqrt{\frac{4\beta_e A^2}{V_t\,m}}, \qquad \zeta_h = \frac{K_{ce}}{A}\sqrt{\frac{\beta_e\,m}{V_t}} + \frac{b}{4A}\sqrt{\frac{V_t}{\beta_e\,m}}. \] \(\omega_h\) sets the hard ceiling on closed-loop bandwidth: no controller can push crossover much past it without exciting the lightly damped resonance. Hydraulic systems are notoriously **underdamped** (\(\zeta_h\) of a few hundredths to a few tenths), because the natural leakage and viscous friction that provide \(\zeta_h\) are deliberately kept small to limit losses.

## Consequences for design and control

Because damping is so low, practical actuators rely on added mechanisms, such as valve underlap, deliberate cross-port leakage, or active pressure/acceleration feedback, to lift \(\zeta_h\) into a usable range. Nonlinearities compound the linear picture: stiction and Coulomb friction cause dead-band and limit cycling near zero velocity; the square-root valve characteristic makes gains load-dependent; and end-of-stroke deceleration (cushioning) is a strongly nonlinear event. Pneumatic actuators are governed by the same continuity-plus-Newton structure but with gas thermodynamics replacing the bulk-modulus term, giving them far lower and more temperature-sensitive stiffness and even lighter damping, which is why precise pneumatic servoing is difficult. In practice these models are the backbone of sizing (matching \(\omega_h\) to the required bandwidth), controller synthesis, and time-domain simulation of motion where the actuator drives a mechanism.
