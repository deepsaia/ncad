A board design is maintained in two coupled representations: the **schematic** (the logical intent, in nets and component instances) and the **layout** (the physical reality, in placed footprints and routed copper). Neither is authoritative for everything, and both change over a project's life. **Annotation** is the disciplined synchronization between them, and its correctness is what guarantees that the fabricated board actually implements the drawn circuit. It runs in two directions.

**Forward annotation** pushes information from schematic to layout. The schematic is the source of truth for *connectivity and component identity*: which parts exist, their reference designators and footprints, and how their pins map to nets. When the schematic changes (a part added, a value changed, a net rewired), the difference is transmitted forward, typically as an **Engineering Change Order (ECO)** or netlist import, so the layout gains the new footprints and updated ratsnest without the designer manually re-entering anything. The first forward annotation is simply the initial handoff that seeds an empty board with the schematic's parts and connections.

**Back annotation** pushes information the other way, from layout to schematic, because some decisions can only be made sensibly in the physical domain. The canonical cases are **pin and gate swaps**: two logically interchangeable pins (say the inputs of an AND gate) or two identical gates within a multi-unit package can be exchanged during routing to remove a crossing or shorten a track. That swap changes the pin-to-net mapping, so it must be reflected back into the schematic or the two views diverge. **Reference-designator renumbering** (renaming parts so `R1..Rn` follow board position rather than drawing order) is likewise a layout-driven change fed back to the schematic. The rule of thumb is that layout may reassign *equivalent* resources but must never invent new connectivity; anything that alters intent belongs in the schematic and flows forward.

The governing principle is a maintained invariant: after any exchange, the schematic netlist and the layout netlist must describe the *same* electrical graph. Tools enforce this either by keeping the two databases live-linked and cross-probing every edit, or by exchanging ECO files that each side applies and confirms. When that loop is broken (a designer edits copper without back-annotating, or edits the schematic without forward-annotating) the two representations drift, and the discrepancy surfaces later as a failed comparison or, worse, a board that does not match its documentation. Robust flows therefore treat annotation as continuous rather than a one-time export, and they log every change as a reviewable delta so the history of *why* the physical design diverged from the naive schematic order remains traceable.
