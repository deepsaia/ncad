**Placement** is the act of instantiating a footprint at a specific location on the board: assigning it a position, a rotation, and a side (top or bottom). Each placed component is a footprint definition combined with a rigid-body transform. In two dimensions that transform is

\[ \begin{bmatrix} x' \\ y' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} t_x \\ t_y \end{bmatrix}, \]

with an additional mirror (for example \(x \to -x\)) applied for bottom-side parts, which flips silkscreen, mask, and paste along with copper. The placement record therefore stores position, angle, side, and usually a *locked* flag so that autoplacement or nudging cannot disturb a hand-tuned critical part.

The key clearance construct associated with each placement is the **courtyard**: a keep-out envelope that bounds the space the component and its solder joints occupy, plus an *excess* margin for assembly access. The courtyard is deliberately larger than both the component body and its land pattern. Its purpose is to guarantee room for pick-and-place nozzles, for rework tools, and for adjacent parts, so that two components that individually pass do not collide in practice. Density level sets how much courtyard excess is applied, tightening it for high-density designs and loosening it where rework must be easy.

The primary rule that placements must satisfy is **non-overlap of courtyards**. A design-rule check treats each placed courtyard as a polygon and flags intersections; because the courtyard already encodes assembly margin, a clean courtyard map is a strong first-order guarantee of assemblability. Additional constraints layer on top: mechanical keep-outs (connectors at a board edge, mounting-hole exclusion zones), height/height-profile limits under a heatsink or enclosure lid, and thermal spacing for hot parts.

Placement also carries **manufacturing metadata** beyond geometry. The centroid and rotation of each part feed the assembly machine's pick-and-place file directly, so a consistent origin and rotation convention (typically centroid origin, pin 1 marked, angle measured counterclockwise) is essential; a wrong convention silently rotates real parts on a real line. Fiducials and panelization interact with placement as well, since the board's coordinate frame must map cleanly onto the assembly stencil and machine.

In the broader data model, placements are the **instance layer** that binds the reusable footprint library to a concrete board: they reference a footprint, hold the transform and side, and expose the courtyard for spatial checks. Keeping the transform explicit and the courtyard derived from the footprint means the same library part can be dropped anywhere and still be validated for clearance and assembly without re-authoring its geometry.
