A **footprint** (also called a *land pattern*) is the board-side counterpart of a physical component: the arrangement of copper lands, solder-mask openings, paste-stencil apertures, silkscreen, and a keep-out courtyard that together let one specific package be soldered down and connected. It is the join between the abstract electrical world (a symbol, a net, a pin number) and the physical board. Every placed component instance is, at bottom, a footprint transformed into board coordinates.

A complete footprint carries several coordinated layers of information: the **copper lands** the terminals solder to; **mask openings** (usually a slight swell over the copper) that expose those lands; **paste apertures** (usually a slight shrink) that meter solder paste at assembly; a **silkscreen** outline with a pin-1 marker for visual assembly and rework; an **assembly outline** and reference designator; a **courtyard** keep-out; and an association to a 3D body for clearance and visualization. Pad numbering ties each land back to the component's pin names so nets connect correctly.

The geometry is *derived*, not drawn freehand. Given the package's dimensioned terminals, a land pattern is computed to produce a target **solder joint**: a defined toe fillet (outboard), heel fillet (inboard), and side fillet. The standardized construction combines the component's minimum and maximum terminal spans with fillet goals \(J_T\) (toe), \(J_H\) (heel), and \(J_S\) (side) to yield the land's outer span, inner gap, and width. Tolerances are combined statistically rather than by naive worst-case; a root-sum-square combination

\[ C_F = \sqrt{\,C_L^{2} + F^{2} + P^{2}\,} \]

rolls up component tolerance \(C_L\), fabrication allowance \(F\), and placement accuracy \(P\) so the pad is neither starved nor bridged. Three **density levels** (maximum, nominal, and least, i.e. most/ nominal/ least land protrusion) let the same package be tuned for hand-rework-friendly boards or for high-density boards where courtyards must shrink.

Conventions make footprints portable and machine-usable. The origin is placed at the package centroid (the pick-and-place reference), rotation is defined about that origin, and pin 1 has an unambiguous mark. A footprint destined for the bottom side is *mirrored*, which flips silkscreen and paste along with copper. Because assembly machines consume the centroid and rotation directly, getting these conventions right is as important as getting the pad sizes right.

In the data model a footprint is a reusable **library object** parameterized by package family and lead form, instantiated many times on a board. Treating it as a first-class, versioned entity (rather than loose copper) is what makes design-rule checks, bill-of-materials linkage, and automated assembly output tractable: the same land pattern definition drives the copper, the stencil, the placement file, and the DFM checks from one source of truth.
