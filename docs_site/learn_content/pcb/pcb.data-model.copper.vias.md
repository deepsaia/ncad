A **via** is a plated hole that connects copper on two or more layers of the stackup, letting a net change layers. Structurally it is a padstack (pads on the layers it lands on, antipads on the planes it must clear) bound to a drilled, copper-plated barrel. Vias are what make a multilayer board a three-dimensional connectivity graph rather than a set of isolated planes.

Vias are classified by which layers their barrel **spans**. A **through via** is drilled through the entire finished board (all layers). A **blind via** connects an outer layer to one or more inner layers but does not pass all the way through. A **buried via** connects only inner layers and is invisible from either surface; it must be formed on a sub-stack before final lamination. A **microvia** is a small, typically laser-drilled hole that usually spans a single layer pair in high-density interconnect (HDI) construction; because a single microvia only steps one layer, deeper transitions are built by **stacking** microvias (directly on top of one another, requiring filled-and-planarized lower vias) or **staggering** them.

The dominant reliability metric is **aspect ratio**, the ratio of the length the plating must cover to the hole diameter. For a through hole,

\[ AR = \frac{T_{\text{board}}}{d_{\text{drill}}}, \]

and high aspect ratios starve the barrel of plating in the middle, hurting reliability under thermal cycling; fabricators cap it (commonly around 8:1 to 10:1 for through holes). Microvias are governed by a depth-to-diameter target, typically held near or below 1:1 so the laser-drilled cup plates reliably. These limits, together with the layer span, constrain what stackups can physically host a given via.

The via record must also capture **surface treatment**: whether the via is tented (mask over it), plugged, or fully filled and capped, because these choices affect whether the via can sit under a component pad ("via-in-pad" requires filling and planarizing to avoid solder wicking) and how it behaves in a solder joint. Standardized fill/cover types enumerate the options from bare through fully filled-and-plated. Thermal vias, dense arrays used to conduct heat from a component pad to an inner plane or the opposite side, are a specialized use where fill and count matter for thermal resistance.

At high speed a through via carries a **stub**, the unused length of barrel below the layer the signal actually uses, which resonates and degrades the signal; **back-drilling** removes that stub, and the fact must be recorded per via. In the data model, then, a via is best represented as a padstack plus a start/stop layer pair, a drill, a fill/cover class, and optional back-drill depth, all of which downstream checks read to validate aspect ratio, plane clearances, and signal-integrity rules.
