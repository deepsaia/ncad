A printed circuit board is not a single object but a **laminated stack** of alternating conductive and insulating sheets, bonded under heat and pressure, then finished with surface layers that protect and label the copper. The *stackup* is the ordered, top-to-bottom enumeration of every one of these layers together with its function, material, and thickness. It is the most fundamental object in a board's data model: almost every downstream rule (impedance, clearance, drill spans, thermal behavior, cost) is expressed relative to layer indices in the stackup.

Layers fall into a few functional classes. **Copper layers** carry signals and power; they are further distinguished as *signal* layers (routed traces) or *plane* layers (large continuous power/ground pours). **Dielectric layers** are the insulating substrate between copper: fully cured *cores* (glass-epoxy laminate clad with copper on one or both faces) and *prepreg* (a partially cured, resin-impregnated glass sheet that flows and bonds adjacent layers during lamination). **Solder mask** is the polymer coating (usually liquid photo-imageable) that covers copper except where joints are made, preventing bridging and oxidation. **Silkscreen** (the legend) prints reference designators, polarity marks, and outlines. **Solder paste** is not a fabricated layer at all but a stencil-aperture definition used at assembly. Flex and rigid-flex boards add *coverlay* and adhesive layers.

The governing design idea is **electrical adjacency and mechanical symmetry**. A high-speed signal layer must sit next to a reference plane so its return current has a continuous, low-inductance path directly beneath it; the dielectric thickness to that plane sets the trace's characteristic impedance. Mechanically, the stack should be **balanced**: copper coverage and dielectric distribution mirrored about the centerline so that the differential thermal expansion during lamination and reflow does not bow the finished board. This is why copper-layer counts are almost always even, and why an ad-hoc odd stack tends to warp.

Copper thickness is conventionally specified as *weight* per unit area rather than a linear dimension, because the fabrication process plates and etches by mass. The canonical conversion is

\[ 1\ \text{oz/ft}^2 \;\approx\; 1.344\ \text{mil} \;\approx\; 34.8\ \mu\text{m}, \]

so a "1 oz" outer layer is roughly \(35\ \mu\text{m}\) of copper and a "0.5 oz" inner layer roughly \(17\ \mu\text{m}\). Finished board thickness is the sum of all copper and dielectric layers, and it drives via aspect ratio and mechanical fit.

In a data model the stackup is naturally an **ordered array of records**, each carrying a layer type, a reference to a material definition, a nominal thickness, and (for copper) a weight. Because order encodes meaning, insertions and deletions must renumber consistently, and layer identity should be stable so that traces, vias, and drill spans that reference "layer 3" survive edits elsewhere in the stack.
