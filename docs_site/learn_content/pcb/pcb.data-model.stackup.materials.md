Every dielectric layer in a stackup references a **base material** whose electrical, thermal, and mechanical properties propagate into the board's performance. The dominant family is woven glass reinforcement impregnated with an epoxy resin system (the generic "FR-4" grade), but the model must also accommodate high-glass-transition epoxies, polyimide for high-temperature and flex applications, and low-loss PTFE or ceramic-filled hydrocarbon laminates for radio-frequency work. The material definition is therefore a shared record that layers point to, not a per-layer scalar.

The two electrical properties that matter most for signal integrity are the **relative permittivity** (dielectric constant, \(D_k = \varepsilon_r\)) and the **loss tangent** (dissipation factor, \(D_f = \tan\delta\)). Permittivity sets how fast signals propagate and how thick the dielectric must be to hit a target impedance; the phase velocity in the medium is

\[ v_p = \frac{c}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_{r,\text{eff}}}}, \]

so a laminate with \(\varepsilon_r \approx 4.3\) slows signals to roughly half the speed of light. The loss tangent governs dielectric attenuation, which grows with frequency; a low-\(D_f\) material is what separates an RF-capable substrate from a general-purpose one. Both values are **frequency-dependent** and, for woven-glass laminates, weakly direction-dependent because the resin and the glass bundles have different permittivities (the "glass-weave" or fiber-weave effect).

Thickness in this family is built from discrete building blocks rather than arbitrary values. **Cores** come in standard finished thicknesses; **prepreg** thickness depends on the glass style and resin content and, critically, changes after pressing because resin flows to fill copper voids. Glass styles are identified by fabric numbers (for example 106, 1080, 2116, 7628) that encode the yarn and weave density; a fabricator selects a combination of prepreg sheets to hit both the mechanical bond and the target dielectric spacing. A realistic data model stores *pressed* (post-lamination) thickness for the electrical calculation and lets the fabricator choose the specific prepreg build to achieve it.

Thermal and mechanical properties round out the record. The **glass-transition temperature** \(T_g\) marks where the resin softens and the out-of-plane (z-axis) thermal expansion accelerates; the **decomposition temperature** \(T_d\) marks irreversible breakdown. The **coefficient of thermal expansion**, especially in z, drives plated-through-hole barrel fatigue during thermal cycling, because the barrel and the surrounding resin expand at very different rates. Moisture absorption and thermal conductivity affect reliability and heat spreading. High-\(T_g\), low-CTE materials exist precisely to protect via barrels in thick, lead-free-assembled boards.

Because these attributes feed impedance solvers, loss budgets, and reliability models, the material layer of the data model is best treated as an authoritative library entry with named grades and versioned property values, referenced by the stackup rather than duplicated. That indirection keeps a board consistent when a material's characterized \(D_k/D_f\) is refined, and lets the same physical stack be re-costed against alternative laminates without re-authoring geometry.
