**Electromagnetic compatibility (EMC)** is the ability of an electronic system to operate correctly in its environment without generating intolerable electromagnetic disturbance to other equipment, and without being unduly disturbed by others. It has two complementary faces: **emissions** (the noise a product produces) and **immunity/susceptibility** (its tolerance to noise arriving from outside). Each of these divides again into a **conducted** path (energy traveling along cables and power leads, typically below ~30 MHz) and a **radiated** path (energy leaving or arriving as fields, typically above ~30 MHz). Regulatory limits (defined by bodies such as CISPR and national authorities) cap emissions and mandate immunity, so EMC is both a physics discipline and a compliance requirement.

The coupling that matters most for radiated emissions is **common-mode** current. A differential (signal-and-return) current pair encloses a tiny loop and radiates weakly, but a small common-mode current flowing on an attached cable acts as an efficient antenna. For a short loop of area \(A\) carrying current \(I\) at frequency \(f\), the far-field radiated electric field scales as

\[ E \propto \frac{f^{2}\, A\, I}{r} \]

The \(f^2\) dependence explains why fast edges (rich in high-frequency harmonics) dominate emissions, and why loop area \(A\) is the lever a designer controls: this is the same tight-return-loop principle that governs signal integrity. A **cable driven by common-mode voltage** radiates with the far more dangerous monopole/dipole efficiency, so suppressing common-mode noise on I/O is central to passing radiated tests.

## Near Field, Far Field, and Board-Level Practice

The distinction between **near field** and **far field** sets the boundary at roughly \(r = \lambda / 2\pi\). In the near field, electric (high-impedance) and magnetic (low-impedance) fields are treated separately and shielding effectiveness depends on the field type; in the far field the wave has settled to the free-space impedance \(Z_0 = \sqrt{\mu_0/\varepsilon_0} \approx 377\ \Omega\) and behaves as a plane wave. Test-antenna measurements are made in the far field, but board-level coupling and probe diagnostics happen in the near field.

At the board level EMC is won by the same measures that produce clean SI and PI: keep return paths tight and continuous so loop areas stay small; slow edge rates to the minimum the timing budget allows so the harmonic spectrum rolls off sooner; provide a low-impedance PDN so switching noise does not modulate the planes and escape on cables; and control common-mode current at connectors with **ground stitching, common-mode chokes, filtering, and shielding**. The 20-dB-per-decade content above the corner frequency \(f_{\text{knee}} \approx 0.35 / t_r\) is what appears in the emissions spectrum, so managing rise time and loop geometry addresses both the source and the antenna at once.

EMC also demands protection against external insults: **electrostatic discharge (ESD)**, fast transients, and surges are handled with clamping devices, series impedance, and careful grounding at the point of entry, while good immunity design keeps induced noise from reaching sensitive nodes. Because emissions, immunity, SI, and PI all trace back to loop area, edge rate, and reference-plane integrity, EMC is best treated not as a late-stage fix but as a consequence of disciplined layout from the start.
