Clearance and creepage are the two spacing rules that keep conductors at different potentials from breaking down the insulation between them. They are often confused because both are measured in millimetres between two pieces of copper, but they describe different physical failure paths. **Clearance** is the shortest distance measured *through air* (line of sight) between two conductors. **Creepage** is the shortest distance measured *along the surface* of the solid insulator that separates them. Wherever the insulating surface has a groove, slot, or rib between the conductors, the creepage path must follow that surface contour, so creepage is always greater than or equal to the clearance for the same pair of conductors.

The two rules exist because insulation fails in two distinct ways. Clearance guards against **dielectric breakdown of the air gap**: when the field exceeds the air's breakdown strength the gap ionizes and arcs over, a fast event governed by Paschen's law and therefore sensitive to altitude (thinner air derates the gap). Creepage guards against **surface tracking**: under a combination of voltage, humidity, and surface contamination, a partially conductive carbonized path grows across the dielectric over time. A board can survive its clearance test on day one and still fail by tracking months later, which is why the two are specified separately.

## Governing rules

For bare-board copper, minimum spacing is keyed to the peak voltage between the two nets and to an application category (internal conductors, external uncoated conductors at sea level vs. high altitude, and permanently coated conductors). Up to a few hundred volts the minimum is a small fixed value; above roughly 500 V it grows linearly with voltage, of the general form

\[
s_{\min}(V) = s_{500} + m\,(V_{\text{peak}} - 500),
\]

where the slope \(m\) is smallest for internal conductors and largest for uncoated external conductors exposed to high-altitude air. Creepage is instead a function of the **working voltage**, the **pollution degree** (how much conductive contamination the surface sees), and the **material group** of the laminate, ranked by its Comparative Tracking Index (CTI). A higher-CTI material or a cleaner environment permits a shorter creepage path for the same voltage.

In a design-rule check, clearance is one of the workhorse geometric tests: for every pair of copper objects belonging to *different* nets, the shortest edge-to-edge distance is compared against a rule that may be global or set per net class (a high-voltage isolation net gets a much larger rule than a signal net). Creepage checks add the surface-path constraint and often the requirement that a slot or milled groove physically extend the path across an isolation barrier. These rules matter most at mains-voltage interfaces, isolation barriers between primary and secondary sides, reinforced-insulation boundaries in medical and safety-critical equipment, and any high-voltage analog front end, where an under-spaced gap is both a reliability defect and a certification failure.
