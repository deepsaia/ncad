A **copper zone** (also called a *pour* or *filled area*) is a bounded region of copper on a layer, defined by an outline polygon and usually assigned to a net, most often a ground or power net. Rather than store the filled copper directly, the model stores the *intent* (the outline, the net, and a set of rules) and computes the actual filled shape by **subtracting clearance** around every foreign object the pour must avoid: other-net pads, traces, vias, the board edge, and cutouts. The result is the outline minus a Minkowski-style offset of all obstacles by their clearance.

Zones exist for several reasons at once. A ground pour provides a **low-impedance return path** directly under signal traces, which is essential for signal integrity and radiated-emission control. Power pours distribute current with minimal voltage drop and add plane capacitance. Large copper areas spread **heat**. And on outer layers, balanced copper coverage improves plating uniformity and reduces warp. Because a pour typically wraps around many features, its geometry is inherently derived and must be recomputed whenever the objects it flows around change.

Where a same-net pad connects to a pour, a **thermal relief** is usually inserted: instead of a solid connection, the pad joins the pour through a small number of narrow **spokes** with gaps between them. The purpose is thermal, not electrical: a solid connection wicks so much heat into the surrounding copper during soldering that the joint will not reflow or a hand-solder iron cannot heat the pad. The relief is parameterized by spoke count, spoke width, and gap, and it trades a modest increase in connection resistance for manufacturable joints. High-current connections may instead demand a solid (spokeless) connection, which the rules must allow explicitly.

Zone computation is a **determinism hazard** that the data model must confront directly. The filled result depends on the clearance rules, on the *priority order* when multiple zones overlap on the same layer (which one yields to the other), on minimum-width and island-removal thresholds (orphan copper not connected to the net is deleted), and on the numerical details of the polygon-offset and boolean algorithm. Two different fill engines, or the same engine at different tolerances, can produce subtly different copper for identical inputs. A robust model therefore either treats the pour as *authored geometry* captured once, or pins a single, versioned, deterministic fill so that the same inputs always yield byte-identical copper; otherwise regression tests, geometry hashes, and reproducible fabrication output all become unstable.

In practice the zone record holds an outline, a net, a priority, and a clearance/thermal rule set, and the fill is a cached derived artifact keyed to those inputs. Keeping the intent and the derived fill separate lets the model re-pour deterministically after edits while preserving the designer's declared boundaries and connection strategy.
