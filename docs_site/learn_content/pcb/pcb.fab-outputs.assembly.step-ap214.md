A populated circuit board is a mechanical object as much as an electrical one: it has thickness, mounting holes, tall capacitors, connectors that must line up with a panel cutout, and heatsinks that must clear a lid. To reason about *fit* and *clearance*, the board and its component bodies are exported as a **3-D solid-model assembly** into the mechanical-CAD (MCAD) world. The standard vehicle for that exchange is **STEP** (ISO 10303, STandard for the Exchange of Product model data), which represents parts as precise **boundary-representation (B-rep)** solids organized into an assembly tree with placements and colors -- neutral, vendor-independent, and readable by every mechanical CAD system.

## Why AP214 specifically

STEP is delivered through *application protocols* (APs) that fix which subset of the huge 10303 information model applies to a domain. **AP214** -- "Core data for automotive mechanical design processes" -- is the workhorse AP for mechanical assemblies of solids with color and basic structure, and it is a natural fit for an ECAD-to-MCAD board handoff: the board becomes a solid, each component a positioned solid child. Its newer sibling **AP242** consolidates AP203 and AP214 and adds product-and-manufacturing information (PMI), but its *electrical* scope is oriented toward **wire harnesses**, not the packaged-component board envelope. So for the mechanical representation of a board -- outline, thickness, and component 3-D bodies for spatial checks -- AP214 (or AP242's mechanical subset) is the right target, and the electrical semantics stay in the ECAD domain where they belong.

## What the model carries, and what it does not

The exported STEP assembly carries geometry and structure: the board solid with its true outline and stackup thickness, component bodies at their placed positions and orientations, and per-body color for visual identification. It deliberately does *not* carry copper, nets, or electrical connectivity -- it is a **mechanical envelope**, not a functional model. This is what makes it useful for enclosure fit, connector and light-pipe alignment, keep-out and maximum-height verification, and interference detection against the surrounding machinery in a digital mockup.

## Relationship to lighter-weight exchange

Before full 3-D solids became routine, ECAD-MCAD collaboration used the lightweight **IDF** format, which conveys only the board outline, component placements, and simple bounding heights -- enough for a coarse height/keep-out check but not true geometry. STEP AP214 supersedes that with genuine B-rep bodies, so a mechanical engineer can measure real clearances rather than block approximations. The tradeoff is size and detail: exporting every passive as a faithful solid is heavy, so practical workflows often substitute simplified proxy bodies for small parts while keeping accurate geometry for connectors, tall electrolytics, and anything near an enclosure wall.
